## Transcript

Welcome to Iconoclast Insights. I'm André Daus.

This first episode is foundational — it's about the methodology behind everything I do, and behind everything you'll hear on this podcast. What is Strategic Opposition? Where does it come from? And why does it matter for the kind of work that leaders, founders, and teams actually face?

### The Origin: Military Red Teaming

The intellectual foundation of Strategic Opposition is military red teaming — a methodology developed to stress-test plans, strategies, and operational concepts through structured adversarial challenge.

Red teaming, in its original form, was simple: you assigned a group to try to defeat your plan. Not to improve it. Not to find implementation risks. To defeat it — to find the arguments, the scenarios, and the failure modes that would make the plan fail.

The discipline came from a recognition that the people who designed a plan were structurally ill-equipped to identify its weaknesses. Not because they were unintelligent. Because they were too close to it, too invested in it, and too embedded in the assumptions that produced it.

### The Transfer to Business

What I do is apply this framework to organizational decision-making. The specific context is different — we're not planning military operations — but the underlying problem is identical.

The people designing a business strategy are the people most ill-equipped to identify its weaknesses. They know the industry too well. They've worked on the plan too long. They're too close to the assumptions that produced it to see them clearly.

### Why "Opposition"?

I use the word "opposition" deliberately. It's not "critique" and it's not "red teaming" in the corporate sense that has been defanged and turned into a facilitation exercise.

Opposition means taking the other side. It means finding the best possible case against your strategy and presenting it seriously. It means treating your plan as a hypothesis rather than a conclusion.

The goal isn't to prove that the strategy is wrong. The goal is to find out whether the strategy can survive serious challenge — and if it can, to know why, at a level of specificity that allows you to defend it.

### What This Podcast Will Explore

Each episode will explore one aspect of this discipline — a cognitive bias, a decision-making failure mode, a practical technique, or a case study from the kind of work I do.

The unifying thread is this: the problem is rarely the problem. The situation that isn't improving isn't improving because of the surface-level issue you're addressing. It's improving because of an assumption underneath that issue that hasn't been examined.

That's where we're going. Let's start.
