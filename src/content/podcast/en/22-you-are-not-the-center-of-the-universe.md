Welcome to a new episode of Iconoclast Insights.

I've been watching people make decisions lately—in boardrooms, in strategy sessions, in their daily lives. And I've noticed something troubling.

Most of what we call decision-making isn't decision-making at all. It's elaborate theater designed to maintain an illusion that's costing us everything.

Today, we're going to examine that illusion—and what becomes possible when you finally abandon it.

If you think you're making decisions... if you believe you're in control... if you're convinced that when you speak, the world rearranges itself according to your will—

You're living in a dangerous delusion.

The uncomfortable truth is, your environment shapes you far more than you could ever shape it. You're not the conductor of this orchestra. You're barely even playing second violin.

## The Ego's Grand Performance
I hear it constantly in boardrooms, in strategy sessions, in daily conversations: "I decide this." As if the universe holds its breath, waiting for their pronouncement.

But here's what they don't grasp—decisions only exist when circumstances force them into existence. Without external pressure, without environmental demands, there would be nothing to decide. You think you're steering, but you're actually responding to forces that were already in motion long before you showed up.

Most of what we call "decisions" aren't decisions at all. They're elaborate self-deceptions we use to maintain the illusion of control.

## The Myth of Daily Decisions
Someone once claimed we make 30,000 decisions every day. What spectacular nonsense.

Getting out of bed isn't a decision—it's a biological imperative. No one in human history has consciously decided to remain horizontal forever. Choosing between the blue shirt or the green one? That's not a decision; it's a meaningless preference with zero consequence.

Filing your tax return versus not filing it? Please. Unless you're actively seeking government-sponsored misery, there's no choice to be made. Calling this a "decision" is like claiming you "decided" to breathe this morning.

We've convinced ourselves that we're constantly choosing, constantly directing our lives, when in reality we're simply following the path of least resistance through a landscape we didn't create and don't control.

## When Giants Fall: The Kodak Delusion
Even corporate titans fall prey to this cosmic narcissism.

Kodak invented the digital camera in 1975. Then they "decided" not to pursue it because, after all, they were a chemical company. Film was their identity, their expertise, their destiny.

The truth? The market, technology, and consumer behavior had already decided Kodak's fate. While they held internal debates about strategic direction, the world was moving past them at light speed. They thought they were making strategic decisions from a position of strength.

They weren't deciding anything. The environment—technological evolution, changing consumer preferences, competitive pressures—was deciding for them. Kodak's executives believed they were the captains of their ship, but they were already passengers on someone else's vessel.

This wasn't a strategic miscalculation. It was a fundamental misunderstanding of their position in the ecosystem. They mistook their role as controllers when they were merely participants in a much larger game.

## The Dangerous Comfort of Control
Why do we cling so desperately to this illusion? Because accepting our true position—as responsive elements in a vast, interconnected system—feels diminishing. It threatens our sense of importance, our narrative of personal agency.

But this resistance costs us everything.

When you operate from the assumption that you're the center of the universe, you stop paying attention to the actual forces shaping your reality. You make decisions based on internal preferences rather than external dynamics. You optimize for ego satisfaction rather than environmental alignment.

As a result, you get blindsided by changes you didn't see coming, overwhelmed by forces you didn't acknowledge, and defeated by systems you refused to understand.

## The Transition: From Controller to Navigator

Now, this doesn't mean we're powerless. It means we're powerful in ways we haven't been taught to recognize.

The most effective people I work with share a common characteristic: they've abandoned the fantasy of control in favor of something far more potent that I call „environmental intelligence“.

They don't try to change the wind. They learn to read it with extraordinary precision.

But here's the challenge: most of us are trapped in the narrow corridor of our own perspective. We see the world through the lens of our experience, our role, our biases. We mistake our limited view for the complete picture.

This is where most decision-making fails—not because people lack intelligence or good intentions, but because they're working with incomplete information, filtered through a single viewpoint.

## The Method: Multiple Perspectives, Deeper Intelligence
There's a technique I use in every high-stakes meeting, borrowed from military tactical planning but applicable anywhere decisions matter:

"Nobody speaks twice until everybody spoke once."

Here's how it works: Everyone contributes their perspective on the issue at hand. We start with the person lowest in the hierarchy—the one least likely to have their voice heard otherwise. Each person speaks once. No interruptions, no immediate rebuttals, no dominant personalities hijacking the conversation.

Only after everyone has contributed do we move to the second round.

This isn't feel-good democracy. This is tactical intelligence gathering. Different positions in an organization, different roles, different backgrounds—they all provide different windows into the same reality. The person closest to customers sees different patterns than the person managing cash flow. The newest team member notices things that veterans have stopped seeing.

When you combine this approach with the first question from the Navy SEALs' Six Strategic Questions—"What problem are we actually trying to solve?"—something remarkable happens.

Suddenly, everyone realizes they haven't been solving the same problem. They've been operating with different definitions of the challenge, different assumptions about the objective, different interpretations of success.

This is the moment of truth. This is when the illusion of unified purpose dissolves, and you see the actual complexity of your situation.

## Why This Works: Intelligence Versus Ego
The beauty of this approach isn't that it's more democratic—though it is. The beauty is that it's more intelligent.

When you gather multiple perspectives systematically, you're not just being inclusive. You're triangulating reality from different vantage points. You're building a three-dimensional understanding of your environment instead of operating with a flat, single-perspective view.

This makes your responses to environmental pressures more sophisticated, more nuanced, more likely to succeed.

You're still responding to forces beyond your control—market dynamics, technological changes, competitive pressures, regulatory shifts. But you're responding with better information, clearer understanding, and more strategic awareness.

The ego wants to be right. Intelligence wants to be effective.

## Back to Kodak: What Multiple Perspectives Reveal
Kodak had people who understood digital photography's potential. They had engineers who saw the technological trajectory. They had market researchers who could read consumer behavior patterns.

But one voice dominated—the voice that said, "We're a chemical company. This is who we are."

If they had systematically gathered perspectives from different parts of their organization, different market positions, different technological viewpoints, they might have seen what was actually happening in their environment. They might have realized that being a "chemical company" was becoming irrelevant to their customers' needs.

Instead, a single perspective—rooted in historical identity rather than environmental reality—determined their response to a changing world.

## The Practice: From Insight to Implementation
[Pause for emphasis]

This isn't abstract philosophy. This is practical methodology.

Here's what I want you to do this week: Take one significant challenge you're facing—a business decision, a strategic choice, a complex problem that matters.

Instead of approaching it from your default perspective, ask the foundational question: "What problem are we actually trying to solve here?"

Then gather perspectives. If you're leading a team, use the "nobody speaks twice" method. If you're working solo, deliberately seek input from people in different roles, with different stakes in the outcome, with different ways of seeing your situation.

Listen not just to what they say, but to what they reveal about the forces actually shaping your environment.

You'll discover that what you thought was a clear decision becomes something more complex and more interesting. You'll find that your "obvious" solution was obvious only from your particular vantage point.

## The Fundamental Shift: From Center to Navigator
The shift from believing you control your environment to understanding how to navigate it isn't just tactical—it's philosophical.

You stop wasting energy trying to make reality conform to your preferences. You start investing that energy in reading reality more accurately and positioning yourself more strategically within it.

Sailors don't change the wind—they position their sails to harness it. Skilled surfers don't create waves—they read water patterns and ride the energy that's already there.

The most successful people I know aren't those who impose their will on the world. They're those who develop extraordinary sensitivity to environmental forces and exceptional skill at positioning themselves advantageously within those forces.

## Your New Operating System
Stop asking: "How do I make this happen?"

Start asking: "What's actually happening here, and how do I position myself intelligently within it?"

Stop thinking: "I need to decide this."

Start thinking: "What are the forces at play, and what response would be most aligned with reality?"

Stop believing you're the center of the universe around which everything else revolves.

Start recognizing yourself as a sophisticated participant in a complex system—one who can influence outcomes precisely because you understand your actual position and power.

The paradox is profound: The moment you stop trying to control everything is the moment you become truly effective at influencing what matters.

Your environment will continue shaping you. The question is whether you'll develop the intelligence to shape your response.

The universe doesn't revolve around you. But once you understand your actual place within it, you can navigate with precision, respond with wisdom, and achieve outcomes that pure ego never could.

That's not a limitation. That's liberation.
