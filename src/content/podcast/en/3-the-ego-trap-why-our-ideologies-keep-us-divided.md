Welcome back to Iconoclast Insights, where we dig into the messy, fascinating undercurrents of our world. I'm André Daus, and today we are diving into something that's been gnawing at me lately. The way our societies seem stuck in a loop of division, fueled by ideologies that promise answers, but eventually only deliver conflict.

We are talking about truth, diversity, power, and the one thing that ties it all together. Ego. Buckle up, because this one's going to get under your skin.

Let's start with a question. What is an ideology? At its core, it's a set of ideas.

A lens we use to make sense of the world. It tells us what's real, what's right, and what we should do about it. Think capitalism, socialism, liberalism, or even newer flavours like vogue culture or techno-optimism.

30 years ago, the ideology landscape looked different, less fragmented, maybe less loud. Today, it feels like every week spawns a newism. Each one claiming to have the upper hand on truth.

But where do these labels come from? Some are self-named. Liberalism screams freedom.

Others get slapped on later. Like neoliberalism, the term that's more accusation than identity. And then there's the current wave.

Progressism, postmodernism, cultural conservatism. Names let's stick, because they capture a vibe, a fight or a feeling. Now I've been watching this shift, and two threads stand out.

The rise of my truth and the obsession with diversity. Both are tied to what we call postmodernism and vogue ideology. Ideas that sound noble but often trip over themselves in practice.

Take my truth. It's a phrase you hear everywhere now. A rejection of one size fits all facts.

It comes from this postmodern idea that truth isn't fixed. It's a perspective, shaped by who you are, where you've been. That's fair enough.

We all see the world through our own eyes. But here, here's where it gets dicey. When my truth becomes a shield, it stops being a perspective and starts being a weapon.

Suddenly, it's not about understanding reality together, it's about defending your corner against everyone else's. And when that happens, we are not far off from fake news, just with better branding. Then there's diversity.

It's the buzzword of our age, plastered on corporate websites, university mission statements, political speeches. On paper, it's about inclusion, giving everyone a seat at the table. But let's be real.

The way we talk about it today is less about unity and more about settling scores. It's not just about bringing people in. It's about pushing others out, rebalancing power based on who's been up or down.

And here's my provocation. I don't think diversity is about skin colour, gender or any of that. Diversity happens between your ears.

It's in your head. It's about how you think, not what you look like. A room full of people who look different but think the same?

That's not diverse. That's an echo chamber with better optics. Meanwhile, a group that looks identical but disagrees on everything?

That's where the real sparks fly. So why does this matter? Because we are losing the ability to talk about it.

We are so busy shouting my truth or policying who's diverse enough that we've forgotten how to actually argue. Not to win, but to learn. And I think it's because we are trapped in an ego game.

See, ideologies don't just explain the world. They protect us. They give us a tribe, a flag to wave, a reason to feel superior.

On a personal level, it's one person saying, I'm right and you're not. Zoom out, and it's a group saying, we are the good ones, they are the problem. Zoom out even further, and it's an entire society splitting into us versus them.

Left blames right, right blames left, climate folks blame capitalism, CEOs blame the activists. Everyone's pointing fingers, waiting for the other side to blink first. And the longer we do that, the deeper the divide gets.

Let me paint you a picture. Imagine a debate. Any debate.

Someone step up and says, this is how it is. Boom, the anchor set. That first claim, that opening shot, it's like a stake in the ground.

Psychologists call it the anchoring bias. Whatever hits first, sticks hardest. From there, the whole conversation bends toward that frame.

Facts? They come later, if at all. And they are fighting an uphill battle against that first impression.

Now add ego to the mix. The person who set the anchor isn't just defending an idea. They are defending themselves.

Challenge them, and it's not a discussion anymore. It's a war. And the group behind them?

They are not about to let their champion lose. Before you know it, you've got a movement, an ideology, and a counter movement. All because someone wanted to be right.

Here is where it gets brutal. We are all guilty of this. Me, you, the person you are mad at right now.

We've all got skin in the game. Our egos love a good fight. It feels good to be part of something, to know you are on the right side.

But what if that's the trap? What if the real problem isn't the other side's ideology? It's that we are all too attached to ours?

Think about it. Every time we dig in, every time we say, they need to change, we are feeding the same cycle. It's like a pirate council.

Everyone voting for themselves, yelling over each other, until the whole thing gets a mess. In Pirates of the Caribbean, Jack Sparrow flips the script. He's got the power to vote for himself, but he gives it to Elizabeth instead.

One move, and the chaos stops. Ego takes a backseat, and something shifts. Now, I'm not saying we all need a Jack Sparrow moment, though it would make a hell of a story.

In the real world, it gets messier. Power doesn't just vanish when you give it up, someone else grabs it. For that pirate trick to work, you'd need the right person, someone with enough clout to matter, and the right timing, and a thousand other stars to align.

You'd need multiple people stepping back at once, not just one hero. And even then, the next guy in line could just crank up the ego machine right back up again. It's a long shot.

The kind of thing that looks great in a movie, but feels impossible when you're scrolling through X, watching people tear each other apart over the latest outrage. So, where does that leave us? Honestly, I don't have the answer.

And I'm not here to pretend I do. But I'll leave you with this. Maybe the first step isn't about fixing the world out there.

Maybe it's about looking at ourselves. Not the big, dramatic, I- renounce-my-power moment. Most of us don't have that kind of sway anyway.

I mean the small stuff. The next time you're in an argument, online or off, ask yourself, am I trying to understand or am I trying to win? Am I open to being wrong or am I just waiting for them to cave?

Because if we can't break that ego habit in ourselves, we've got no shot at breaking it anywhere else. Truth isn't objective. Not really.

It's a status, a snapshot seen from a million angles. Diversity isn't about who's in the room. It's about what's in their heads.

And power? It's only as good as what we do with it. We are stuck in this loop because we keep playing the same game.

My truth, those is yours. My group, those is theirs. Maybe it's time to stop pointing at the other guy and start asking, what am I holding on to and why?
