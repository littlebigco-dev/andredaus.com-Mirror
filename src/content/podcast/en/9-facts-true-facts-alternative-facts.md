## INTRO

Welcome to Iconoclast Insights – the podcast that isn't afraid to challenge your most cherished assumptions.

I'm André Daus, and today we're diving into something so fundamental, so seemingly straightforward, that almost no one bothers to question it: the concept of facts.

What exactly is a fact?

Why do we invoke them to shut down conversations rather than open them up?

And perhaps most provocatively – are there really such things as "alternative facts"?

Stay with me for the next fifteen minutes. I promise you'll never hear the phrase "That's just a fact" the same way again.

## THE ANATOMY OF A FACT

Let's start with a deceptively simple question: What is a fact?

A fact feels solid, immutable, immovable. Something that simply... is. Period. End of discussion.

But I want to ask you – can you define what a fact is in a way that holds true across all worldviews, all times, all cultures, all contexts?

You probably can't. Neither can I.

At its core, the word "fact" merely means "something that has actually happened or is the case." But who determines what "actually happened"? Who decides what "is the case"?

The philosopher Ludwig Wittgenstein once wrote: "The world is the totality of facts, not of things." But he didn't say whose facts. From which perspective. Through which lens.

In reality – and especially in political or social debates – a "fact" is rarely an objective rock amidst the crashing waves of opinion. Rather, it's an interpretation that has gained dominance within a particular worldview, reinforced by authority, repetition, and social consensus.

Think about it. How many times have you heard phrases like:

"The fact of the matter is..."
"It's simply a fact that..."
"The facts speak for themselves..."

Each time, the speaker isn't just offering information. They're asserting dominance. They're saying: "My understanding isn't just one way of seeing things – it's the way things are."

But is it?

## THE SUBJECTIVE GAZE

Imagine a letter drawn in the sand on a beach. Two people stand on opposite sides, looking at this same symbol.

One says: "That's clearly an M."
The other insists: "No, that's clearly a W."

Who's right?

Most of us would say: "It depends on your perspective."

But what if both individuals are absolutely convinced they're seeing a fact? What if both say: "It's a fact that this is an M" and "It's a fact that this is a W"?

This isn't just a thought experiment. This is the essence of so many of our cultural and political divides.

Consider immigration. One side looks at the data and sees facts that prove immigration strengthens economies. The other side looks at the very same data and sees facts that prove immigration creates economic burdens.

Same numbers. Different perspectives. Different "facts."

And here's where we hit the real problem:

The invocation of facts isn't designed to clarify – it's designed to terminate.

We don't say: "Let's look together at what we're both seeing."
Instead, we say: "I see the facts – you're seeing incorrectly."

And once we've labeled our perspective as "fact," we've effectively armored it against questioning. We've removed it from the realm of discussion and placed it in the realm of the indisputable.

This is why so many conversations – about climate, about economics, about politics, about identity – end with both sides claiming the other is "ignoring the facts."

But what if the real ignorance lies in not recognizing that our facts are filtered through our perspectives? That what we call facts are actually interpretations that have been solidified by our values, experiences, and assumptions?

What if the most intellectually honest position isn't "I have the facts," but rather "Here's how I'm interpreting what I see"?

## THE POWER OF FACTS

Facts aren't just claims about reality. They're instruments of power.

When someone says, "I have the facts on my side," what they're really saying is, "I have authority. I have legitimacy. I have the right to be heard and believed."

Daniel Pink once ironically noted in a TED talk how lawyers in Washington DC would use the phrase "a true fact" – as if some facts might be more factual than others. It seems absurd on the surface, but it betrays something profound about how we use the concept.

Facts are hierarchical. Some facts are deemed more important, more reliable, more factual than others. And who controls this hierarchy? Those with the power to determine which facts matter.

This brings me to perhaps the most notorious phrase of our recent political era: "alternative facts."

When Trump's spokesperson Kellyanne Conway used this term in 2017 to describe contradictory claims about inauguration crowd sizes, the reaction was swift and severe. Journalists, politicians, and commentators decried it as Orwellian doublespeak – an attack on objective truth itself.

And perhaps it was. But perhaps it also inadvertently highlighted an uncomfortable reality: that what we accept as facts are often just established viewpoints, sanctified by repetition and authority.

When someone challenges our established facts, we call it "fake news" or "misinformation" – even though it might simply be a different reading of the same M in the sand, viewed from another angle.

I'm not suggesting there's no such thing as truth or that all claims are equally valid. But I am suggesting that the line between fact and interpretation is far blurrier than our confident assertions would have us believe.

And when we use "fact" as a weapon to silence dissent or as a shield to protect our worldview from scrutiny, we're not engaging in the pursuit of truth. We're engaging in the exercise of power.

## FACT-CHECKING AS FAÇADE

So what about fact-checking? Surely that's a solution to our problem – an objective method to separate fact from fiction, truth from falsehood?

It certainly sounds good. Verify claims. Provide context. Create clarity.

But all too often, fact-checking feels performative rather than illuminating.

The typical fact-check follows a simple format:

1. Claim (usually quoted verbatim)
2. Examination (selected evidence and counter-evidence)
3. Verdict (true/false/misleading/partly true)

This gives us the comfort of certainty – but not necessarily the gift of understanding.

Because even complex statements with layers of context, assumption, and interpretation get reduced to a simplistic true/false binary.

And who decides which sources are valid? Which data points matter? Which contexts are relevant and which are distractions?

At worst, fact-checking becomes a form of intellectual gatekeeping – a bouncer at the door of truth, admitting only the guests who match the venue's preferred profile.

This isn't to say we should abandon fact-checking. But perhaps we should approach it with more humility and transparency:

"Based on these sources, which we consider reliable for these reasons, this claim appears to be inconsistent with the available evidence."

That's a lot less satisfying than "FACT CHECK: FALSE" – but it's also a lot more honest.

Because the pretense of absolute certainty doesn't serve truth – it undermines it by making us less willing to revise our understanding when new information emerges.

## A NEW WAY FORWARD

So where does this leave us? If facts aren't the objective anchors we thought they were – if they're shaped by perspective, wielded for power, and often reduced to simplistic verdicts – how do we navigate our conversations, our politics, our shared reality?

I suggest a few principles:

First, we need to recognize that there are no truly objective facts – only shared perspectives. This doesn't mean abandoning the pursuit of truth. It means pursuing it with more humility and awareness of our own filters.

Second, we need to stop using the word "fact" as a power move. Instead of saying "It's a fact that," try saying "From what I understand" or "Based on the evidence I've seen."

And third, we need to develop a new conversational culture. Not fact-checking, but fact-discussing. A practice where we say:

"This is how I see it – and here's the information I'm basing that on. How do you see it, and why?"

This approach doesn't diminish truth – it elevates it by recognizing its complexity.

It acknowledges that the path to better understanding isn't through asserting our facts more loudly, but by exploring how different perspectives might each contain pieces of a larger truth.

Imagine if our political debates, our social media arguments, our dinner table discussions operated this way – not as battlegrounds where facts are weapons, but as laboratories where different viewpoints help us refine our collective understanding.

What if, instead of saying "That's just a fact," we started saying "That's how I currently understand it, and I'm open to seeing more"?

It would require more effort. More vulnerability. More intellectual honesty.

But it might also lead to less polarization, more nuanced thinking, and ultimately, a healthier relationship with truth itself.
