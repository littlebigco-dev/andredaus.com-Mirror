Welcome to Iconoclast Insights. I'm André Daus, and this is the final episode of 2025.

Instead, to ask why we keep playing this annual theater of self-improvement when the calendar flipping from December 31st to January 1st is just... Wednesday followed by Thursday.

Let me start with a question: If you genuinely wanted to change something in your life – really change it – would you wait until January 1st to start? Or would you start the moment you decided it mattered?

The answer tells you everything you need to know about New Year's resolutions.

## The Arbitrary Nature of Calendar-Based Change
Here's the uncomfortable truth: January 1st has no magical properties. It's not a cosmic reset button. It's an arbitrary point on a human-made calendar system that we've collectively agreed to treat as significant.

Think about it this way. In business, we have these things called "jour fixe" meetings – regular status meetings every Monday or Friday. They exist because we need structure and rhythm. But imagine if someone said: "I'll only address this critical problem during our Monday jour fixe, not today on Thursday when I discovered it."

You'd think they were ridiculous.

Yet that's exactly what we do with New Year's resolutions. We discover something about ourselves in October – maybe we're not exercising enough, or we're working too many hours, or we've been meaning to learn that new skill. And instead of starting immediately when the motivation is high and the problem is clear, we... wait. We wait for an arbitrary calendar date to give us permission to change.

Why?

Because we've been conditioned to believe that change requires a "fresh start." A clean slate. A symbolic beginning.

But here's what that really means: We're outsourcing our commitment to a calendar date instead of taking responsibility for it right now.

## The Cultural Ritual That Replaces Real Commitment
New Year's resolutions have become a cultural ritual. And like most rituals, the performance often matters more than the outcome.

We announce our resolutions to friends, post them on social media, maybe even write them down with elaborate goal-setting frameworks. We feel good about it. We feel like we're doing something. And in that feeling, we often find enough satisfaction that we never actually follow through.

The ritual itself becomes a substitute for the work.

Let me give you a personal example. Back in episode zero, when I launched this podcast, I told you that I planned to add video eventually, and later add conversation episodes. That wasn't a resolution. That was a plan. I didn't announce it because it felt good to announce it. I announced it because it was already in motion. The decision was made. The timeline existed. The work had begun.

And guess what? In January 2026, we're launching video. Not because it's a new year, but because that's when the preparation is complete. The conversations will come too – longer format episodes where I challenge assumptions with guests, not bound by the 10-15 minute solo format.

You see the difference? I didn't need January 1st to give me permission. I needed a plan, resources, and execution. The date is incidental.

When you make a real commitment, you don't wait for permission from a calendar. You start building the infrastructure to support that commitment immediately. You don't announce it to feel good. You announce it when it's already happening.

## The Social Performance Versus Genuine Change
Here's where it gets really interesting. New Year's resolutions aren't just about us lying to ourselves. They're often about performing change for others.

We say we're going to "get healthy" or "be more productive" or "spend more time with family" because these are socially acceptable goals that make us look good. They're aspirational in the safest possible way.

But how many people make genuinely uncomfortable resolutions? How many people say: "This year, I'm going to stop pretending to care about networking events I hate" or "I'm going to quit the hobby I've invested years in but secretly despise" or "I'm going to stop chasing a promotion I don't actually want"?

Those kinds of resolutions don't get applause. They don't fit the narrative of self-improvement. They're about subtracting things that make us look successful rather than adding things that sound ambitious.

And that tells you what's really happening: We're performing change, not committing to it.

Genuine change is often messy, unglamorous, and socially awkward. It's telling a client no. It's walking away from a business opportunity that looks good on paper but feels wrong. It's admitting you were wrong about something you've been doing for years.

Those changes don't wait for January 1st. They happen in the middle of a random Wednesday when you finally have enough of your own bullshit.

## The Real Question
So let me bring this back to where we started: New Year's resolutions.
If you're making resolutions this year, ask yourself three questions:

One: Why am I waiting until January 1st? If this actually matters, what's stopping me from starting today?

Two: Am I making this resolution because I genuinely want to change, or because it's what I think I should want to change?

Three: Am I committing to the work, or am I just performing the commitment?

Those questions will tell you everything you need to know about whether your resolution will last past February.

And if you decide you genuinely want to change something – don't wait for the new year. Don't wait for Monday. Don't wait for the "perfect time." Start building the infrastructure today. Make the first call. Block the calendar. Delete the app. Send the message. Whatever it is.

Because here's the truth: The difference between December 31st and January 1st is exactly the same as the difference between Wednesday and Thursday. It's just another day.

What changes isn't the calendar. What changes is your commitment.
And commitment doesn't need permission from a date.
