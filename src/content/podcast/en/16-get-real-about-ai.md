## The Fairy Tale of the Fragile Creator

Welcome to Iconoclast Insights – your podcast for straight talk, critical thinking, and change with conviction.

I'm André Daus – coach, consultant, and someone who prefers building boats over yelling at storms.

Today, we’re diving into a topic that’s less about AI and more about accountability: why so many people demand progress without disruption, comfort without change, and income without ownership.

It’s not just a tech debate—it’s a cultural symptom. And it’s time we confronted it head-on.

There’s a German saying I can’t stop thinking about in this respect:
“Wasch mich, aber mach mich nicht nass.”

Roughly translated to “Wash me, but don’t get me wet.”

It perfectly captures the whining I hear right now—from creators, publishers, startups, consultants—anyone who feels threatened by AI.

“We want the benefits of change—just not the discomfort.”

“Sure, we want traffic, reach, sales, growth… but only through channels we already know and control.”

Sorry. That’s not how change works. The environment shifts. You adapt—or you get out of the way.

## This Isn’t New — You’ve Seen This Story Before

Let me take you back to 2019.

A friend of mine—let’s call him Jens—was a taxi driver in Cologne. One of the good ones. Took pride in his car, offered water, even had Wi-Fi before it was trendy.

Then Uber rolled into town. The taxi ranks exploded in protest. “It’s unfair! It’s illegal! They’re stealing our jobs!”

You know what Jens said?

“They’re not stealing my job. They’re just showing how little value we’ve added for years.” That’s what he literally said „They’re just showing how little value we’ve added for years.”

He didn’t join the protest. He adapted. Registered for Uber, learned how the app worked, and kept his clients by building actual relationships.

Now fast forward: journalists said the same when blogs gained traction. Newspapers blamed Google. Hotels panicked at Airbnb. Retailers fought Amazon.

And now content creators are pointing fingers at AI.
But the truth is: this isn’t theft—it’s evolution.

## Traffic Isn’t a Currency—It’s a Lease Agreement

Let’s break down the myth:
“AI companies should pay creators for scraping content!”

Okay, maybe. But then let’s rewind. Did Google pay you when it indexed your site?

No.

It gave you traffic—and you called that payment.

But traffic is like foot traffic in a mall:

You don’t own the mall, you don’t set the rent, and you sure as hell don’t complain when someone walks past your store.

Traffic is attention. It’s potential. It’s not income unless you know what to do with it.

Too many creators relied on AdSense or affiliate revenue from casual visitors. They never asked: “What’s my real value here?”
They just built castles on borrowed ground.

And then came AI—and pulled the ground out from under them.

## The Fragility of “Old Reliable” Business Models

This is where the story turns inward.

A lot of creators, entrepreneurs, and even consultants built fragile models—models that only work under very specific conditions:
	•	High SEO ranking
	•	Ad-supported monetization
	•	Social media virality
	•	Platform dependency

Here’s a dirty little secret:

If you need 100,000 monthly visitors to make your business work, your business is broken.

If you need to “go viral” to get clients, your offer isn’t strong enough.

If you need a middleman platform to generate value, you’re not offering real value.

That’s not sustainable. That’s hope as a strategy.

And when someone automates part of what you do—or compresses your value into a sentence—you either show why you’re still essential… or fade away.

## Storytelling Time — The Boutique Consultant Who Thrived

Let me tell you about someone who didn’t panic.

A client of mine—let’s call her Sara—ran a tiny online education business. She made videos, templates, tools. Niche stuff. Small audience.
Then ChatGPT came out, and her industry panicked: “Nobody needs our courses anymore! They’ll just ask the AI!”

But Sara didn’t flinch. She asked: “How can I use this tool to serve my clients better?”

She started integrating AI prompts into her courses. Built GPT-ready templates. Taught clients how to co-create with AI rather than compete against it.

Guess what? She 3×’d her business in a year.

Because she understood this:

AI didn’t replace her. It replaced lazy competition.

## What to Do Instead – Practical Takeaways

Alright. Let’s get to the actionable part. Here’s what you do when the tide turns:

1. Own Your Audience

Stop renting reach.
Build an email list. Build a real community. Own the relationship.

If your entire audience lives on Instagram, LinkedIn, or Google—you’re not the owner, you’re the tenant.

2. Build Real Products

Make something that solves a problem. Not just infotainment. Not just content for clicks.
A product. A workshop. A service. A transformation.

AI can summarize your blog post. It can’t deliver your lived experience.
That’s your moat.

3. Redefine “Value”

Instead of asking: “How can I monetize attention?” ask:

“What transformation do I create—and for whom?”

People don’t buy blog posts. They buy clarity. They buy certainty. They buy speed, trust, and outcomes.

4. Use AI as Your Amplifier, Not Your Enemy

Create with it. Automate boring stuff. Use it to get faster.
But never let it replace your thinking.

The most dangerous people in business right now are those who know how to think with AI—not against it.

## No More Excuses

Let’s bring it back full circle.

“Wash me, but don’t get me wet.”
That’s the motto of people who want the benefits of progress without the discomfort of change.

But real leaders don’t whine.
They don’t blame.
They adapt.

So ask yourself: Are you yelling at the storm—or learning how to sail?

Because this isn’t going away.
And you don’t get to choose the waves.
But you do get to choose your boat.
