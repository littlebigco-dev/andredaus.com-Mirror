Welcome to Iconoclast Insights. I am André Daus and this episode will be a bit different from the others. 

I want to talk about consequences today. Not in the moralizing sense—not as punishment or karma—but as something far more basic: con-sequence. A sequence. If you start something, something else will follow. Every action changes something. After that action, the world is different. And that different doesn't have to be positive. It can have negative results, side-effects, downstream impacts. And whoever did the action is responsible for what follows.

Sounds simple, right? It's not.

Here's what's been bothering me: I keep watching people—smart people, capable people—take actions that cause obvious damage, and they genuinely don't see the connection. I'm not talking about butterfly effects or chaos theory. I'm talking about straightforward causation. The manager who constantly berates his team and wonders why morale is terrible. The person who never maintains relationships and is confused why they're alone. These aren't complex system dynamics. These are direct cause and effect.

And when you point it out to them? Nothing. They don't see it. Or worse, they deny you ever warned them.

So let me walk through what I've been thinking about this, because I don't have clean answers—but I think I'm getting closer to understanding the mechanism.

## The Speed Excuse

First, let's deal with the most common excuse I hear: "Everything is moving faster today. We don't have time to think about consequences like we used to."

That's bullshit.

Time hasn't accelerated. What's changed is cognitive load and attention fragmentation. People have more inputs, more noise, more options—so they've trained themselves to react faster and think less. They've confused speed of response with quality of decision-making.

But here's the thing: people have always been bad at connecting actions to distant consequences. What's changed is that consequences arrive faster and more visibly than they used to, which creates the illusion that everything is moving faster. The speed hasn't changed. The visibility of your mistakes has.

## Why Showing Isn't Seeing

But even when you slow down, even when you point directly at the causal chain—"you did X, which caused Y"—people often can't accept it. And I think I understand why now.

Showing someone something isn't the same as them seeing it.

There are three mechanisms at work:

**First, their mental model doesn't include that connection.** They have a story about how the world works, and your causal chain doesn't fit their story. So they reject the connection—not because they're stupid or stubborn, but because accepting it would require rebuilding how they understand cause and effect in that domain.

**Second, identity protection.** If accepting "I caused this bad outcome" threatens how they see themselves—as competent, as good, as smart—their brain will work overtime to not see the connection. This isn't conscious. It's automatic defensive processing.

**Third, they lack the conceptual framework.** You can see the connection because you have mental categories that link those concepts. They don't. It's like pointing at a chess position and saying "white is lost in seven moves." A novice can't see it even when you show them—they lack the pattern recognition.

Don’t get me wrong, none of those is bad. It is what it is.

## The Narration Problem

Now here's what I think is the deeper issue: People don't observe their own behavior—they narrate it.

The manager doesn't experience himself as "berating." He experiences himself as "holding people accountable" or "maintaining standards" or "being direct." Those are different actions in his internal narrative. When morale drops, there's no connection to "holding people accountable"—that's a good thing! So he looks elsewhere for the cause.

The person who doesn't maintain relationships doesn't experience themselves as "neglecting people." They experience themselves as "busy" or "focused" or "independent." When they end up alone, it doesn't connect to those self-described traits.

The gap between self-perception and external reality is the blind spot.

And here's why it's so hard to fix: To see the consequence, they'd first have to see their action accurately. But seeing their action accurately threatens their self-concept. So their brain protects them from seeing it.

It's not that they can't trace cause and effect. It's that they can't see the cause as it actually is—only as they need it to be.

## The Society-Centric Shift

There's another angle I've been considering. I think modern education has become more society-centric than it used to be—teaching children to prioritize group harmony, consensus, not standing out, being considerate.

But ego doesn't disappear when you teach people to suppress it. It goes underground.

You end up with people who have egos but can't acknowledge them. They've been taught that having strong individual desires, seeing themselves as central actors, or prioritizing their own judgment is somehow problematic. So when they act from ego—which everyone does—they can't recognize it as such.

And here's where this connects back to consequences: If you can't acknowledge your ego as a causal force, you can't see yourself as a causal agent.
Society-centric thinking teaches "we" instead of "I." But "we" is diffused responsibility. "We" made the decision. "We" need to improve. Nobody individual caused anything—it's all collective.

Which means nobody is actually responsible for consequences. It's always systemic, structural, cultural—never "I did this specific thing and it caused that specific result."

## The Opposition Paradox

So what do you do about this? That's where I keep hitting a wall.

Strategic Opposition—the work I do—only works if people let it. You cannot force someone to be their own opponent. And the people who most need to examine their thinking are precisely the people who don't see the value in it.

They don't opt in because they don't believe they have blind spots. And they don't believe they have blind spots because... they have blind spots about having blind spots. It's recursive ignorance.

The CEO who says "I want someone to challenge our strategy" is already halfway there. The one who says "we just need better execution" is unreachable—until something breaks badly enough that they can't ignore it.

Most people only learn about consequences after they've already suffered them. The touching-the-stove model. They need the pain before they'll pay attention to how stoves work.

## The Cassandra Problem

And that brings me to what really bothers me: watching people I value make avoidable mistakes despite my warnings.

You can point at the falling domino. You cannot make them see it. You cannot make them care. Your responsibility ends at clear communication—it does not extend to their reception of it.

But when it's people you care about? That's not frustrating. That's painful.

And there's no clean answer. Because the closer you are to someone, the less your warnings land as strategic insight and the more they land as judgment or threat. The very people you're best positioned to help are often the ones most resistant to hearing it from you specifically.

You can't be both close to someone and their opposition. The roles conflict.

## What I'm Left With

So where does this leave us? With uncomfortable clarity but no solution.
We understand the mechanism: self-authorship creates a gap between perceived action and actual behavior. Identity protection prevents people from seeing their role in outcomes. Society-centric education may make it harder to acknowledge individual causation.

But understanding the mechanism doesn't give you a tool for fixing it.
Maybe the insight isn't "how do I reach them" but accepting that you can't. They're going to learn through pain—or they won't learn at all. You can't save people from themselves.

What you can do: Document your warnings. Accept the limits of your influence. Stop taking on their consequences as your failure. And when it's people you value who won't listen? That's just life. People who value you back stay close. Those who never valued you move farther away.

It's not satisfying. But it might be true.
