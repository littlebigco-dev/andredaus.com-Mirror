You know what bothers me? Not the fact that people face difficult problems. Not even that they struggle to solve them. What bothers me is that they've stopped trying to think at all.

There are two German sayings I've been thinking about lately. The first one goes: "Alle haben gesagt, dass es nicht geht. Dann kam einer, der das nicht wusste und hat es einfach gemacht." Everyone said it couldn't be done. Then someone came along who didn't know that, and just did it.

The second one is even simpler: "Geht nicht, gibt's nicht." There's no such thing as "can't be done."

These aren't just motivational poster material. They're observations about a fundamental shift in how people approach problems. And that shift? It's terrifying.

We've entered an era of intellectual outsourcing. People have delegated their thinking to whoever shouts the loudest, whoever has the most followers, whoever packages their ideas in the shiniest buzzwords. And the moment something requires actual thought, actual analysis, actual effort to understand? They throw up their hands and declare it impossible.

Let me give you two recent examples from my world that illustrate this perfectly.

## The AI Circus
A few weeks ago, I was in a discussion about AI optimization for independent businesses. The topic itself is valid – understanding how AI can actually help your business is important. But the conversation? It was a masterclass in non-thinking.

- „We need AI agents!" someone proclaimed.
- „Absolutely, AI agents are the future!" another chimed in.
- „Everyone's implementing AI agents!"

You know what nobody asked? Why. Why do you need AI agents? What problem are they solving? What are they actually doing that you can't do another way? What's the cost-benefit analysis? Do you even understand what an AI agent does?

Silence.

Because asking "why" requires thinking. It requires challenging the premise. It requires admitting that maybe, just maybe, you don't actually need the shiny new thing everyone's talking about. Which is OK!

This is what I call buzzword compliance. People hear a term repeated enough times by enough people, and suddenly it becomes gospel. The thinking process stops at "other people say this is important, therefore it must be important for me too."

No analysis of your actual needs. No evaluation of alternatives. No consideration of implementation costs or complexity. Just... compliance with the loudest voices in the room.

## The GDPR Comedy
Now let me give you the flip side – the "it's too hard" brigade.
In the WordPress community, there's been endless discussion about GDPR compliance, particularly around user data and media handling. And I mean endless. Forum threads stretching into infinity, people complaining, pointing fingers, demanding that plugin developers do all the work.

- „It's impossible to be GDPR compliant!"
- „This is too complicated!"
- „Someone else needs to fix this!"

Meanwhile, I just... did it. Implemented GDPR-compliant solutions across twenty WordPress sites. Local-first AI workflows. Secure data handling. User privacy protection. The whole thing.

Was it hard? No. It required thinking about the actual requirements, understanding the technical constraints, and implementing solutions. That's it. No magic. No impossibility. Just work.

But here's the thing – most people never even got to the work part. They stopped at "everyone says this is hard." They outsourced their thinking to the collective complaint chorus and declared it impossible.

Geht nicht, gibt's nicht. There's no such thing as "can't be done." There's only "I haven't thought about how to do it yet."

## The Pattern: Thinking by Consensus
So what's happening here? In both cases – the AI hype and the GDPR despair – people have replaced thinking with consensus-seeking.

In the AI example, the consensus is positive: "This is good, we need this." So people adopt it without thought.

In the GDPR example, the consensus is negative: "This is hard, it's impossible." So people give up without thought.

Notice the common thread? No independent analysis. No critical evaluation. No actual engagement with the problem itself.

People are ordering their reality by volume. The loudest voice wins. The most repeated phrase becomes truth. The most common complaint becomes insurmountable obstacle.

This isn't thinking. This is intellectual herd behavior.

And it's getting worse. Because now we have another layer: things that sound good get automatic credibility, regardless of whether they're actually useful.

- „AI optimization" sounds sophisticated. So it must be good.
- „GDPR compliance" sounds complicated. So it must be hard.

The actual reality? Irrelevant. The sound of the words has replaced evaluation of the concepts.

## The Complacency Problem
Here's what really worries me: this isn't laziness. Laziness I can understand. Laziness I can work with.

This is complacency. Intellectual complacency.

People have become comfortable not thinking. They've accepted that thinking is someone else's job. The expert's job. The loud person's job. The consultant's job. Anyone's job but theirs.

And when you suggest they might want to think about something themselves? You get resistance. Not "I don't have time" resistance. Not "I need help" resistance. But "why should I have to think about this?" resistance.

As if thinking about your own business, your own problems, your own solutions is somehow an unreasonable ask.

This is how businesses stagnate. This is how innovation dies. This is how entire industries get disrupted by someone who simply didn't know something was "impossible."

You know that first German saying? "Everyone said it couldn't be done, then someone came along who didn't know that and just did it." That's not a cute story about ignorance being bliss. It's an indictment of learned helplessness.

The people who said it couldn't be done weren't stupid. They'd just stopped thinking. They'd accepted the consensus. They'd ordered their reality by the volume of the voices telling them "no."

The person who did it? They just thought about the actual problem.

## What Actually Works
So what's the alternative? It's almost embarrassingly simple.

Start thinking again.

Not complicated thinking. Not overthinking. Just... thinking.

When someone tells you that you need AI agents, ask why. Ask what problem they solve. Ask how they differ from other solutions. Ask what the tradeoffs are.
When someone tells you something is impossible, ask if they've actually tried. Ask what specific obstacles exist. Ask if those obstacles are actually insurmountable or just inconvenient.

When everyone is saying the same thing, ask if they're all thinking independently or just echoing each other.

This isn't skepticism for skepticism's sake. This is basic due diligence. This is what thinking actually looks like.

And here's what I've found: most "impossible" things aren't impossible at all. Most "necessary" things aren't necessary. Most consensus opinions haven't been thought through.

The GDPR implementation I mentioned? Not complicated. Just required actually reading the requirements and implementing them systematically.

The AI agent question? Legitimate use cases exist, but they're specific, contextual, and definitely not universal. Thinking through your actual needs reveals whether you're one of them.

The pattern holds across everything. When you replace consensus with thinking, problems become solvable. When you replace volume with analysis, clarity emerges.

## The Challenge
So here's my challenge to you: What have you accepted as impossible that you haven't actually thought about?

What have you adopted because everyone else said you should, without asking why?
What problem are you outsourcing to the crowd when you could just think it through yourself?

I'm not saying you need to solve everything alone. I'm not saying expertise doesn't matter. I'm saying that before you declare something impossible, before you jump on the latest bandwagon, before you accept the consensus – think.

Actual thinking. Not "what does everyone else say" thinking. Not "what sounds good" thinking. Actual engagement with the actual problem.

Because here's the truth: most of your competitors aren't thinking either. They're following the same voices, accepting the same limitations, chasing the same buzzwords.

Which means the moment you start actually thinking, you have an advantage.
Not because you're smarter. But because you're actually using your brain while everyone else has outsourced theirs.

Geht nicht, gibt's nicht. There's no such thing as "can't be done."
There's only "I haven't thought about it yet."

So start thinking.
