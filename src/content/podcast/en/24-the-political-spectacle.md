Welcome to Iconoclast Insights, where we challenge conventional wisdom and ask the uncomfortable questions that most people prefer to avoid. I'm your host, and today we're diving into something that's been gnawing at me during these local election campaigns here in Germany – and frankly, it's a problem that extends far beyond our borders.

## The Marketing of Democracy

Picture this: Streets lined with campaign posters featuring faces you've never seen, promising things so vague they could mean anything to anyone. "For more climate protection." "For better mobility." "For improved schools." Some don't even bother with substance – just "Vote CDU 3 times" with no image, no hint, no actual content.

This isn't politics anymore. This is brand marketing disguised as democracy.

We once criticized Donald Trump for conducting politics through Twitter in 140 characters or less. Yet here we are, watching our own politicians compress their entire political vision into even fewer words. What's happening to political discourse? Has the pursuit of power completely consumed the purpose of governance?

## The Illusion of Competence

Here's where things get interesting. The political establishment loves to argue that direct democracy – citizen referendums and popular votes – are dangerous because ordinary people lack the expertise to make complex decisions. But this misses the real structural problem with our representative democracy.

Politicians operate within electoral cycles of 4 to 5 years. They make decisions knowing that the next government can simply reverse them. This creates a fundamental disincentive for long-term thinking. Why invest political capital in a 15-year infrastructure project when your successors might cancel it for ideological reasons?

The result is that truly strategic decisions – the kind that shape a nation's future – become impossible within our current system. Politicians focus on what they can achieve and take credit for within their limited timeframe, leaving the hard, long-term choices to chance or to whoever happens to be in power next.

This isn't about questioning representative democracy itself – Germany's parliamentary system has many strengths. But it has a blind spot when it comes to decisions that transcend political cycles. That's where direct democracy, as practiced in Switzerland, offers a crucial complement: when citizens make fundamental choices through referendums, those decisions gain a democratic legitimacy that survives changes in government.

## The Swiss Exception

Switzerland proves this assumption wrong daily. Swiss citizens regularly vote on complex issues – from nuclear energy to immigration policy – and somehow their country hasn't collapsed into chaos. In fact, it's one of the most stable, prosperous nations on Earth. Their system creates something our representative democracies lack: genuine long-term stability.

When Swiss citizens decide on fundamental directions through referendums, politicians can't simply reverse these decisions with the next election cycle. Politicians become implementers of citizen-chosen directions rather than ideological crusaders pursuing their personal visions of how society should function.

## The American Mirror

Cross the Atlantic, and you'll see our problems magnified. American politics has become the ultimate exercise in empty branding. "Make America Great Again" – what does that actually mean? "Build Back Better" – better how, exactly? These aren't policy platforms; they're emotional triggers designed to bypass rational thought.

American voters increasingly vote for feelings rather than policies, for tribal identity rather than practical governance. The result? A political system that lurches between extremes every few years, unable to maintain any consistent long-term strategy. Sound familiar?

## The Real Problem: Ego Over Impact

Here's what I've observed after years of watching this circus: Most people who enter politics do so for the role, not the results. They want to be seen making decisions, giving speeches, wielding influence. They don't necessarily want to solve problems – because solving problems is hard, often unpopular work that may not show results within a single electoral cycle.

I once had a fascinating conversation with a university professor who trained teachers. When I suggested using more career-changers and professionals as educators to address teacher shortages, his response was revealing: "People become teachers because they have a specific image of what they want to do. They're not thinking about how to improve education – they're thinking about how they want to conduct classes, usually based on their own school experiences. They're proud of their lengthy training and don't want anyone challenging their exclusive claim to the profession."

This same dynamic infects politics. Politicians aren't primarily motivated by solving societal challenges – they're motivated by occupying the role of problem-solver. The distinction is crucial.

## The Parking Lot Parable

Let me share a perfect example of this dysfunction in action. Years ago, during a local election, the Social Democrats promised more parking spaces. Neighbors were excited – who doesn't want convenient parking? Many voted for this promise. But there was a fundamental problem: there was literally no space for additional parking.

When I asked supporters where these magical new parking spaces would appear, the response was telling: "Doesn't matter, as long as there are more parking spaces. We don't know what to do with our cars."

Fast-forward to today: the same Social Democrats are systematically eliminating parking spaces to force people onto public transportation – which, by the way, barely functions in Cologne due to staff shortages, aging infrastructure, and constant disruptions.

This isn't hypocrisy – it's something worse. It's policy-making based on ideological fashion rather than practical problem-solving. First, they promised what people wanted to hear. Now, they're implementing what they think people should want, regardless of whether the alternative systems actually work.

## The Marketing Trap

Modern politicians claim they've learned from consumer marketing, but they've actually learned the wrong lesson entirely. In consumer marketing, the cardinal rule is: if you try to appeal to everyone, you appeal to no one. Successful brands identify their target audience and speak directly to their specific needs and values.

Politicians do the exact opposite. They craft messages designed to offend no one and appeal to everyone. "Better schools" – who could disagree? "More climate protection" – sounds universally positive. "Improved mobility" – everyone wants that.

But this approach, which politicians think makes them safer, actually makes them weaker. When you try to please everyone, you end up pleasing no one authentically. Voters sense the calculated vagueness and lose trust. They know they're being marketed to rather than spoken to honestly.

Real leadership requires making choices that some people won't like. It means saying, "This is what I believe is right, even if it costs me votes." The attempt to avoid all controversy, to find the magical message that satisfies every voter, is precisely what makes political communication so hollow and unsatisfying.

## The Long-Term Catastrophe

Here's the deeper problem: Everything important – climate policy, infrastructure, education reform, economic transition – requires thinking beyond electoral cycles. But our democratic systems reward short-term thinking and punish long-term vision.

Look at Germany's energy policy disaster. Politicians have spent years debating whether to turn nuclear power plants on or off, as if these were household appliances you could switch at will. The reality? Nuclear facilities require years of planning, training, and preparation to safely start up or shut down. You can't operate complex energy infrastructure based on the whims of electoral politics.

But the real damage isn't just technical – it's the massive uncertainty this creates for businesses and citizens. Companies can't invest in long-term energy strategies when the fundamental rules change with every election. Families can't plan their energy costs when policy zigzags every few years. The constant political theater around nuclear power has created exactly the kind of instability that makes rational economic planning impossible.

This pattern repeats across policy areas: grand announcements followed by reversals, leaving everyone – businesses, institutions, and ordinary citizens – unable to plan for a future that keeps changing direction every electoral cycle.

## The Path Forward

So what's the solution? First, we need to admit that our current systems aren't producing the leadership we need. Representative democracy, as currently practiced, has become a popularity contest between marketing teams rather than a serious mechanism for governance.

We need institutional changes that reward long-term thinking and punish shallow populism. This means:

Constitutional provisions that protect fundamental policy directions from political whiplash. Let citizens vote directly on major long-term strategies – energy policy, infrastructure priorities, education philosophy – and require supermajorities to change course.

Term limits for career politicians who've never worked outside the political system. Fresh perspectives from people who've actually built businesses, taught students, treated patients, or solved real-world problems.

Greater transparency in policy-making processes, so citizens can see the real trade-offs and constraints that politicians face, rather than the sanitized versions presented during campaigns.

## The Individual Response

But institutional change takes time, and we can't wait for politicians to voluntarily limit their own power. Each of us has immediate choices to make:

Stop rewarding empty slogans with your support. When politicians speak in vague generalities, demand specific plans. When they promise everything to everyone, recognize the manipulation.

Think beyond tribal loyalties. Political parties are brands, not belief systems. Your values shouldn't be determined by marketing departments.

Engage with the actual consequences of policies, not just their stated intentions. The road to societal dysfunction is paved with well-intentioned policies that ignore practical realities.

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Here's what the political establishment doesn't want you to realize: You're probably more qualified to make decisions about your community's future than most of the people currently making those decisions for you. You live with the consequences. You understand the trade-offs. You have practical experience with the systems that policies are supposed to improve.

The idea that ordinary citizens are too ignorant for direct democracy is itself ignorant. It assumes that political expertise is more valuable than lived experience, that theoretical knowledge trumps practical wisdom.

## The Choice Before Us

We're at a crossroads. We can continue accepting political leadership that prioritizes ego over impact, marketing over meaning, short-term popularity over long-term solutions. Or we can demand something better.

This isn't about left versus right, conservative versus progressive. This is about competence versus incompetence, authenticity versus manipulation, genuine service versus self-service.

The choice is ours – but only if we're willing to think for ourselves rather than letting political marketers think for us.

Real change begins when we stop being consumers of political brands and start being active participants in democratic governance. It starts with asking uncomfortable questions, demanding real answers, and refusing to be satisfied with slogans designed to bypass our critical thinking.

The future belongs to those brave enough to move beyond the safety of empty promises and embrace the risk of meaningful action.
