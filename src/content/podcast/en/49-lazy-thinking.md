Welcome back to Iconoclast Insights. I’m André Daus, and today I need to get something off my chest.

I was listening to music recently — the Boomtown Rats, Banana Republic — and while the song is specifically about something else entirely, one phrase stuck with me. The idea of a place that calls itself modern, that performs progress, that wraps itself in the language of advancement — while underneath, absolutely nothing has changed. The same hollow power structures. The same people making the same bad decisions. Just with a new coat of paint and a fancier vocabulary.

And I thought: that is exactly what I keep running into in business.

We have built ourselves a Banana Republic of thinking. And most people in it don’t even know they’re living there.

## The Performance of Intelligence

Here’s what a Banana Republic of thinking looks like in practice.

Someone walks into a room — a consultant, a LinkedIn personality, a manager who just got back from a conference — and they start talking. And they use the right words. Digital transformation. Simplicity. Agility. AI-driven. Lean. Human-centered. Frictionless. And people in the room nod. They nod because the words sound right. They sound like progress. They sound like someone who has done the work, who understands what the future looks like, who is ahead of the curve.

But nobody stops to ask the obvious question: what are we actually trying to achieve here?

Because if you asked that question — if you genuinely pressed the person at the front of the room to tell you what success looks like, in concrete terms, by a specific date, measured by something observable — the nodding would stop. And the silence that followed would tell you everything.

This is the core of what I’m calling the Banana Republic of business thinking: the performance of intelligence without the substance of it. The vocabulary of progress without the logic of it. It looks modern. It sounds advanced. But the underlying decision-making is as lazy and circular as it has ever been.

## Two Words That Broke Business

I want to talk about two words specifically, because they are the ones I hear most often used as substitutes for actual thinking.

The first word is *digital*.

Someone says “we need to go digital” and the room erupts. Of course. Digital. Obviously. Who could argue with digital? It sounds like the future. It sounds like they’ve been paying attention. But digital what? Digital how? Digital in service of which outcome? Nobody asks. Because asking would interrupt the feeling — and that feeling, that sense that something modern is happening, is apparently worth more than a clear answer.

I have watched companies pour enormous amounts of money and time into “going digital” with no defined destination. They digitized their broken processes. They put their chaos in the cloud. They automated workflows that should have been eliminated. And when it didn’t work — when the outcomes were exactly as muddled as they were before — nobody called it what it was. They just picked a new buzzword and started the cycle over.

The second word is *simple*.

Simple has become the laziest idea in business. Everyone wants simple. Simple processes, simple products, simple communication. And I understand the instinct — complexity for its own sake is a real problem. But simplicity as a goal, without defining what you are simplifying toward, is just an excuse to not do the thinking.

When someone tells me they want a simple solution, my first question is always: simple relative to what? Simple for whom? Simple at which point in the process? Because the things that feel simple on the surface are often brutally complex underneath. And the things that look complex can be the most elegant paths to the result you actually want.

Simple and digital are not strategies. They are not answers. They are aesthetic preferences dressed up as direction. And as long as people keep accepting them as direction, we are going to keep getting outcomes that nobody planned for, nobody wanted, and nobody can explain.

## What Laziness Actually Costs

Now, I want to be precise about what kind of laziness I’m talking about, because laziness gets misunderstood.

I’m not talking about people who don’t work hard. Many of the people who fall into this trap work incredibly hard. They are busy, they are diligent, they fill their days with activity. What they are not doing is the one kind of thinking that actually matters: thinking about what they want to happen, and whether the path they are on has any chance of taking them there.

That is intellectual laziness. And it is far more expensive than physical laziness, because it costs you the results you could have had.

It works like this. Defining a desired outcome is uncomfortable. It requires specificity. It requires you to commit to something. And the moment you commit to something specific, you create the conditions under which you could be wrong. You could fail. You could be judged. So instead of doing that, you reach for a word — digital, simple, agile, innovative — that sounds like an answer without requiring you to actually give one. The word absorbs the discomfort. The room responds positively. And you move forward without ever having decided where forward is.

The cost of this is not theoretical. Every project that runs over budget because the goal kept shifting. Every tool that got implemented because it was “best practice” and not because it solved a defined problem. Every strategy deck that got presented, applauded, and then quietly forgotten because nobody could connect it to anything operational. That is the price of reaching for a buzzword instead of doing the thinking.

## The One Question That Changes Everything

I work with a principle that is deceptively simple — and I use that word deliberately, because it genuinely is simple to state, but it requires real discipline to execute.

The principle is this: start with the desired result.

Before you choose a method. Before you pick a tool. Before you decide on a consultant or a framework or a technology. Before any of that — what does success look like? What is the specific outcome that would tell you, unambiguously, that this worked?

If you can answer that question clearly, everything that follows becomes a search problem. You are looking for the path, or a path, that has a reasonable chance of taking you there. Maybe there are several. Maybe the obvious one is not the best one. But you are operating in the domain of logic now. You can evaluate options. You can reject approaches that won’t work. You can recognize when something is taking you off course.

If you cannot answer that question — if the best you can do is gesture at a word like “digital” or “simple” — then you are not ready to make decisions yet. And the worst thing you can do is pretend otherwise.

This is what I keep telling people. Not as a complicated methodology. Not as a framework with seventeen steps and a certification attached to it. Just: what do you want to happen? Can you say it clearly? Good. Now let’s find the way.

## Why People Don’t Listen, And Who This Is For

I want to be honest about something, because this episode is partly me working through my own frustration.

I know I am not going to change the people who have built their careers on sounding smart. I know I am not going to walk into a room and convince someone who has been nodding at buzzwords for twenty years to suddenly start asking hard questions. That is not realistic, and frankly it is not my job.

But there is a specific group of people I am talking to right now. And if you are still listening, there is a reasonable chance you are one of them.

You are someone who has sat in those rooms. You have watched the nodding happen. You have felt the discomfort of being the person who wants to ask what success actually looks like, and sensing that the question would not be welcome. You have watched decisions get made on the basis of what sounds right rather than what is right. And it has cost you — time, money, momentum, trust in the people who were supposed to know what they were doing.

You are not crazy. Your frustration is not a personality problem. You have been watching a Banana Republic operate and being told that the dysfunction is sophistication.

The way out of the Banana Republic is not a revolution. It is a question. Ask it every time. What are we trying to achieve? What does success look like? How will we know if this is working?

Ask it in the meeting. Ask it of the consultant. Ask it of the agency. Ask it of yourself, before you reach for the comfortable word instead of the true answer.

The people who can’t answer it will reveal themselves quickly. And the people who can are the ones worth working with.

## Closing

That is what I wanted to say today.

Banana Republics — the political kind and the business kind — survive because people mistake the performance of progress for the real thing. They hear the right words and they feel like something is happening. And that feeling is enough to keep the system going, even as the outcomes quietly fail.

You don’t have to be part of that system. You just have to be willing to ask the one question that makes the whole apparatus uncomfortable: what are we actually trying to achieve?
