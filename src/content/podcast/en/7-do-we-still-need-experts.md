Welcome to another episode of Iconoclast Insights. I'm André Daus, and today's topic might make a few people uncomfortable, which is perfect, because discomfort is where thinking begins. And this isn't an episode filled with answers.

In fact, I want to do something dangerous here. I want to question the expert. Not a particular one, but the idea of the expert.

The pedestal, the spotlight, the way we worship certainty. And before we begin, let me be clear. I'm not here to say expertise is worthless.

But I'm here to say the word expert has been watered down to the point of meaninglessness. And worse, it's become a tool of manipulation. Let's start with this.

What even is an expert? Think about the last time you heard someone introduced on stage or on a podcast or in an article. This is John Doe, expert in whatever.

Maybe you thought, oh great, someone who knows what they are talking about. Or maybe you thought, finally, I can stop thinking for myself for a few minutes. And that right there is the problem.

We have confused knowledge with authority. We don't just want someone who knows. We want someone who tells us what to do, so we don't have to carry the weight of uncertainty.

And that's what the expert provides, an escape hatch from thinking But let me ask you something uncomfortable. What if the expert is wrong?

What if they are biased? What if they are just performing confidence? Because let's be honest, confidence is what gets labelled as expertise these days.

Not depth, not humility, not results, just confidence. And in a world obsessed with quick takes, the boldest voice wins, not the wisest one. Years ago, I worked on a project that seemed simple.

Track and reconcile foreign exchange rate changes across departments. No lives on the line, no billion dollar stakes, just a routine business process. But the project dragged on.

And on. And on. I asked a stakeholder one day, what if we start fresh, use everything we've learned and build something clean, something that actually works?

That made sense to him. But the project manager pulled me aside and said, please don't bring that up again. Why?

Because he liked the project. It gave him importance. He was the expert, the one who knew.

And even though the process was broken, completely dysfunctional, it stayed that way. Because the perception of expertise was more valuable than the outcome. Eventually, I was taken off the project, because I wasn't senior enough, as they said.

Because I had the audacity to question the sacred structure. Let that sink in. I wasn't removed for being wrong.

I was removed for asking questions. That's how powerful the expert label can be. It becomes armor, and in some cases, a weapon.

Now, let me get personal. People sometimes introduce me at conferences as an expert in... well, I don't even know what anymore.

Whenever that happens, my first sentence is always, I'm not an expert, I'm just someone who thinks a lot. And people laugh. Or not.

Or both. And they like the humility. But here's what I've noticed.

They only like it as long as it doesn't affect them. The moment someone starts to feel uncomfortable, then they start to realize, I might be challenging something, they believe, they disengage. That's the paradox.

People say they want to think critically, until it means questioning themselves. Then suddenly, it's, you're being too intense, or maybe don't overthink it. But thinking isn't overthinking.

It's resistance to being spoon-fed. And we need more of that. Let's go back to Einstein for a moment.

There's this quote everyone loves. Make things as simple as possible. It's printed on posters, used in keynotes, even written into management frameworks.

But most people leave out the ending. But not simpler. That's the uncomfortable part, because it reminds us that some things shouldn't be simplified.

And yet, that's what we do. All the time. In business, just follow the process.

In medicine, just take the diagnosis and move on. In politics, just trust the expert. We crave certainty more than we crave truth.

And that's a dangerous place to be. I like to break problems down like this. There is simple, like a recipe, a checklist, a clear path.

Anyone can follow it. Then there is the complicated domain. You need expertise, years of training, like fixing a watch.

And then there is the complex domain. No clear path. Everything depends.

Systems interact. You don't solve it. You navigate it.

Most of life is complex, but we treat it like it's simple, or pretend it's just complicated, so we can call in the expert. You see the problem? We use the wrong tools.

We ask for the wrong help, and we reward the people who give us quick answers, not the ones who help us hold the question longer. Now, let's go back to the watchmaker. A real one.

Not a YouTube hobbyist. Someone who has spent years with tiny gears in springs, who can feel a problem that others can't even see. That's real expertise.

Built, earned, felt. Not granted by title. Not manufactured by a brand.

Not invented for a LinkedIn headline. That kind of expert is valuable, but rare. And quiet.

Which is why we don't always recognize them. They don't shout. They don't market.

They don't need to. Because their work speaks. Now compare that to the thought leader, who hasn't built anything, who regurgitates the same carousel content over and over, because that's what get clicks.

We are in a crisis of death, and I'm tired to pretending it's fine. So, here's the real question. Do we still need experts?

My answer? Yes. But only the right kind.

We don't need declarative experts. We need generative experts. People, who think with us, not for us.

Who teach us to think, not to tell us what to believe. Who challenge us to hold paradox, not offer premature clarity. So, maybe the real challenge isn't about experts at all.

Maybe the real question is, do we still want to think? Because once we stop thinking, we'll take anyone who offers an answer. No matter how shallow, no matter how wrong, no matter how dangerous.

I don't want to be an expert. Not in that modern, sterile sense of the word. But I do want to be someone who helps others think.

That's the community I want to build. Not of followers, but of fellow questioners. People who see the real work is rarely in answers.

It's in staying in the question. It's in wrestling with uncertainty. It's in getting uncomfortable and being okay with that.

So maybe this is a manifesto. Or maybe it's a provocation. Either way, I leave you with this.

Next time, someone calls themselves an expert. Ask why. Ask what they have built.

Ask what they have broken. Ask what they have changed their mind about. And then ask yourself the same.

Thanks for listening. If this made you think, it's good. If this makes you uncomfortable, it's even better.

I'll see you in the next one. Until then, stay curious, stay bold, and stay thinking.
