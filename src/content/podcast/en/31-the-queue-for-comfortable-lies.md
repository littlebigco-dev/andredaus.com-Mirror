Welcome to Iconoclast Insights. I'm André Daus, and if you're here for validation or reassurance that everything's fine as it is, you're in the wrong place.

Today, we're talking about a picture. You've probably seen it online—a cartoon with two service counters. One sells uncomfortable truths. Nobody's queuing there. The other sells comfortable lies. The line stretches to the horizon.

Every time I see that image, I think: This is my daily reality.

Let me tell you why.

## THE OBSERVATION

Over the past few weeks, I've been in contact with dozens of agencies and experts. They all tell me how great they are, how innovative, how successful. But the moment you ask too many questions, the façade crumbles into hot air.

You know this, right? It's like the fish market in Hamburg. The vendors are there to sell, not to discuss. Fair enough—that's the fish market. But here's the thing: many companies that aren't on a fish market operate exactly the same way. Ask too many questions, and you'll hear: "If you're not happy, you can go elsewhere."

Or take this classic: Vendors don't claim they have the only solution—they claim they have the best one. Though "best" is always relative to the problem, of course. Often, they'll helpfully offer to adjust your processes for you, so you can get a quick solution that fits their product.

This is the comfortable lie in business clothing. Because it saves us from thinking about the actual problem.

Here's a real example: I recently contacted a major German newsletter provider because many of our emails were bouncing—not landing in spam, but being rejected entirely. I spoke with several hosting providers on the recipient side. They all agreed: the issue was with the sender's authentication settings.

The provider's recommendation? "Just have the recipients whitelist our IPs."

Seriously?

That's like a restaurant saying: "Our kitchen's dirty, but you could pre-sanitize your stomach at home."

## THE PATTERN

This attitude runs through so many areas:

"We've always done it this way."

"What doesn't work, doesn't work."

"Standard is enough."

Behind this isn't ignorance. It's convenience.

Convenience is the currency of our time. It sells better than truth because it feels better. Because it leaves us in peace.

The uncomfortable truth, on the other hand, demands something from us. It says: Think. Take responsibility. Do it right—not just easy.

No wonder its queue is so short.

But here's what fascinates me: Most people join the long queue without thinking. Not because they've evaluated the options, but because so many others are already standing there. They assume: This many people can't be wrong.

Except they're only there because others stood there before them—who also didn't think.

Meanwhile, the comfortable lies get loudly advertised. The providers have enormous reach because they have enormous demand. Their message is: "Look how big we are—therefore what we say carries weight."

When I show up as a one-man operation, I can't compete with that noise. But that doesn't make their weight any more valuable. It's just a bigger pile of shit.

## WHY THIS MATTERS

Let me be clear about something: This frustrates me. Not because I think I'm always right. But because I see how much potential gets wasted.

I work with companies who say they want change. Who claim they want innovation. Who talk about being customer-centric. But when you dig deeper, what they really want is the appearance of change without the discomfort of actually changing.

They want the comfortable lie wrapped in the language of transformation.

Here's what I've learned: Most agencies, experts, and consultants don't sell responsibility. They sell reassurance. They optimize for efficiency and margin, not for truth or impact.

That's why this happens:

When you get specific, they hit their limits.

When you ask questions, they realize you're not a standard client.

When you set your own standards, you become inconvenient.

And when you argue technically or logically, they feel exposed.

The newsletter provider example is textbook: Instead of addressing SPF, DKIM, DMARC, and sender reputation—cause and effect—they sell the workaround. "Just whitelist us." It's like a doctor who says: "Take a painkiller every day instead of finding out what's wrong."

And when I say I do things that others think are impossible, I irritate the system. Because I don't fit in the box.

Mainstream does standard. I do substance.

## THE PERSONAL COST

Here's the part nobody talks about: Being the person at the other counter is lonely.

It feels like you're on the wrong planet sometimes. You start questioning yourself: Am I the problem? Am I too demanding? Too direct? Too much?

I've even asked AI to soften some of my statements. Didn't help. They still don't land well with people who prefer comfortable lies.

Because here's the thing: A factual discussion often becomes personal when you're dealing with black-and-white thinkers. Every question that challenges their worldview feels like an attack—not on their ideas, but on their identity.

I experience this constantly. When I say in a meeting: "Wait, could we be overlooking something important here?"—many don't respond to the question itself. They respond to the implicit message: What you thought was right might be wrong.

And that's unbearable for many people. Because they've tied their identity to their position. They're not just someone who has an opinion. They are that opinion.

So what happens? Instead of thinking together, they shoot back—sometimes below the belt.

"You just want to sound smart."

"You always have to complicate things."

"You're just not a team player."

I call this the defense reflex. It's understandable, but fatal. Because it prevents organizations from learning from their mistakes. It stifles innovation. And it leads to bad decisions because important perspectives get filtered out.

## WHY I DON'T BACK DOWN

So here's my stance: I will not lower my standards to make others more comfortable. Because that would be dishonest—and ultimately ineffective.

If you want real change, you must be willing to question yourself. And if you hire me, you don't get a feel-good program. You get a sparring partner who's allowed to be uncomfortable to create clarity.

Let me tell you something I tell my clients: If your answers come quickly, you've probably asked yourself the wrong questions.

My job is to ask those questions. Even when they're unwelcome.

Because real change begins with irritation. With recognizing that old patterns no longer work. With willingness to step into the unknown—without the security of a pre-packaged answer.

And yes—sometimes I wonder if I'm on the right path with my positioning. But then I remember: The friction I experience isn't a sign I'm wrong. It's proof I'm standing at the boundary between authenticity and adaptation.

Maybe I am on the right path. The discomfort is just confirmation that I'm not selling comfortable lies.

## THE ADVANTAGE YOU DON'T SEE

Here's what the loud majority misses: While they're all standing in line patting each other on the back, there are always people who eventually turn around, look puzzled, and think:

"Wait a minute... something's not right here."

Those are my people. They don't come through advertising. They come through resonance. They sense that I'm not performing.

Yes, it's exhausting to speak against the loud voices. But I have one decisive advantage: Credibility.

It's slow to build but indestructible once it's established. The loud ones must constantly amplify to drown out the fact that they have nothing to say. I just need to stay consistently authentic.

Credibility compounds. Noise just gets noisier.

## Conclusion

So here's what I want you to take away:

Next time you find yourself in a long queue, ask yourself: Why? Are you there because it's actually the right choice—or just because others are already standing there?

And if you find yourself at a different counter, completely alone—stay there.

It's uncomfortable. But that's where progress begins.

The comfortable lie will always have more customers. But the uncomfortable truth will always have more impact.

Choose accordingly.

## Closing

This was Iconoclast Insights.

If this episode resonated with you, share it with someone standing in the wrong queue.

And if you work somewhere that uncomfortable truths aren't welcome—ask yourself how much longer you can stand it.

Until next time: Question everything. Especially the things everyone agrees on.
