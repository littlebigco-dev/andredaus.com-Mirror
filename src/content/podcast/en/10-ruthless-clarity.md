## Intro

Hey there – welcome to the podcast for people who want clear answers, not polite excuses.

I'm André Daus. Today I want to talk about something personal – namely: how I make decisions and why that seems to make so many people... uncomfortable.

Look, this episode isn't for everyone. If you're happy in your mental safe space, maybe skip this one. But if you want to know how to think faster, clearer, and with less concern about making others comfortable – stick around.

## Thinking Beyond Convention
You know, I don't think in bullet points. I don't think in opinions. I think in images – immediately, automatically, without even trying.

When you explain something to me, what happens in my mind is I form this functional model. Not some fantasy – a real schema. Like a system I can test. Every component has to fit. Every connection needs to hold weight.

And when your argument doesn't fit? That image in my head flickers. Something's off. I notice it before I can even put it into words. It's like... there's a short circuit somewhere. It's obvious to me, even when I can't yet explain why.

Most people? They learn to suppress that flicker. They silence that inner voice saying, "Wait, that doesn't make sense," because questioning creates tension. They've been taught that getting along is more important than getting it right – which is a disaster when decisions actually matter.

So yeah, I question things. Directly. Without the small talk. Because I want clarity, not everyone nodding along. This directness isn't me being rude – it's actually respect in its purest form. I respect your intelligence enough to challenge it.

I get it – this comes across as intrusive to a lot of people. But here's the thing: I don't question to make you feel small. I question to figure out if what you're saying holds up – or if it falls apart when taken seriously.

In a world obsessed with feeling good, this is pretty revolutionary: I'd rather be uncomfortable with truth than comfortable with delusion. Not because I enjoy discomfort – but because that's what intellectual maturity looks like.

## My Mind Changes the Picture – Not Me

My thinking works like this algorithm that's constantly correcting itself. I don't need some ideology. No opinion I have to defend forever. When I get new information, the picture just... changes. No drama. No internal debate. Just like that.


## When It Gets Personal, It Gets Serious

I'm not on some crusade here. But there are boundaries:

When false assumptions start influencing decisions that affect me or people I'm responsible for – then it becomes my issue.

This isn't about picking fights; it's about drawing lines. There's a huge difference between tolerating someone's comfortable delusions when they only affect them, versus standing by silently as those delusions spill over into decisions that affect others.

That's when I step into the conflict. Not to win – but to stop nonsense from becoming the standard. Not to prove I'm smarter – but to protect reality from convenient distortion.

Whether it's a competitor with a fundamentally flawed approach getting the contract, or someone affecting my family with thinking that just doesn't work – I can't stay silent. Period.

The alternative is being complicit – watching as poor reasoning leads to bad decisions while saying nothing. Society has trained us to value harmony over accuracy. To nod along with flawed thinking rather than create tension. But that tension – that momentary discomfort – that's exactly where growth happens.

## My Value System: No Coddling
If you want to understand how I operate, here's what I value:

Understanding – not "empathy" in that shallow, modern sense, but genuine comprehension. Not just feeling what someone else feels, but grasping why they feel it, what it means, and whether it connects to reality.

Logic – because feeling something strongly doesn't make it true. Emotions are valid experiences but unreliable guides to reality. They can show us what matters to us, but they can't determine what is.

Responsibility – for impact, not for hurt feelings. For outcomes, not intentions. This brutal accountability to results rather than stories we tell ourselves cuts through all the self-justification that clouds most decision-making.

Truthfulness – even when it hurts. Especially when it hurts. Because temporary pain from facing reality is infinitely better than the lasting damage of acting on illusion.

Loyalty – limited. But deep. Not to positions, institutions, or things I've said before – but to people who've earned it. Not blind allegiance, but considered commitment.

I can tolerate contradictions when they don't matter. But when they lead to real consequences, I'm the first to say: "Hold up – this is wrong." Because otherwise, I become part of the problem.

Most people expect a constant stream of validation. They want to feel understood before they get challenged. I flip that around: I challenge to understand. My questioning isn't separate from my comprehension – it is my comprehension.

## My Decision Model: Clear. Direct. Functional.
What takes most people week-long workshops runs through my mind in minutes. Not because I'm smarter – but because I prioritize differently.

My internal model basically works like this:

What's the actual decision we need to make? Framing matters more than most realize. Most decision paralysis comes from poorly framed choices.

Who's affected – and how much does it matter to them? Not all stakeholders matter equally in every decision. That's not being callous; it's being clear.

What information am I still missing? This question shows the gaps while also preventing that endless hunt for more data that's often just procrastination in disguise.

What options do we have – looking at both logic AND emotion? Logic tells us what could work; emotion signals what matters.

Which of my values are actually at stake here? Not every value comes into play in every decision. Recognizing this allows for more focused thinking.

What happens if I don't decide? Inaction is itself a decision – often the worst one. Recognizing the cost of delay forces accountability that most decision-making processes conveniently ignore.

This isn't some formal "process" I follow – it's automatic thinking. The big difference in my approach is ruthlessly cutting out what doesn't matter. Most decision-making gets bogged down in office politics, imagining how people might react, historical precedents that don't even apply anymore.

I strip all that away. Decision clarity comes from question clarity. When you precisely define what you're actually deciding, half the confusion vanishes instantly.

## What You Can Take From This

Look, you don't have to think like me. But you should become aware of how you think. Most people never examine their own thought processes. They accept the mental patterns they've absorbed through culture, never questioning whether these patterns serve truth or just social convenience.

This unconscious approach to thinking creates a dangerous blind spot. It leaves you open to manipulation, to groupthink, to the comfortable embrace of ideas that feel right but lead nowhere good.

Maybe you recognize yourself in what I'm saying – and feel relieved that you're not "too complicated." That your insistence on clarity isn't rudeness but necessity. That your frustration with fuzzy thinking isn't arrogance but legitimate concern.

We need more people who don't just blow the same horn, but who question the whole orchestra. Who aren't nicer – but clearer. Who don't measure themselves by approval – but by impact.

Clarity isn't cruelty, though it's often mistaken for it. The truly cruel approach is to leave people in comfortable delusion, nodding along with their flawed thinking because confronting it feels too awkward.

The most valuable relationships in your life – personal and professional – will be with people who care enough to tell you what you need to hear, not what you want to hear. Who respect your intelligence enough to challenge it.
