Hello and welcome to Iconocast Insights. I'm André Daus, and today, well, we're talking about control. You know, control is one of those things that people think they understand. We throw around the term all the time—controlling this, controlling that—whenever it seems to fit. But honestly, what do we really have control over? Lately, I've been thinking about this a lot.

Picture this. You're cruising down the highway, hands gripping the wheel, feeling totally in charge. You’re confident, powerful, in control. And then—bam!—someone swerves into your lane, and suddenly, you're slamming the brakes, heart pounding. And in that split second, reality smacks you in the face: You were never really in control. You were just operating within the illusion of it.

Wait… what? An illusion? Really? So, do we actually have control, or is it just something we tell ourselves? And yet, that’s exactly how we approach most of life. We think we’re in control of our employees, our kids, our business strategies—until, well, reality proves otherwise. Usually, when it’s too late.

Today, we’re tearing apart this whole myth of control. We’ll look at why we constantly overestimate our ability to influence others, why we’re so blind to our own behaviors, and—here’s the kicker—why the less you micromanage, the more control you actually have. Yeah, I know, that sounds backward, but stick with me. This might just change the way you think about leadership, parenting, and even yourself.

So, let’s be real for a second. We love the feeling of control, right? It’s comforting. It makes us feel safe. But here’s the thing—we’re not actually controlling most things. What we’re really doing is mistaking influence for control. And that little mix-up? Well, it leads straight to stress, frustration, and—yep—self-sabotage.

Think about the military for a second. A general gives an order: "Take that hill." Now, sure, he controls the command itself. But does he control what happens next? Not really. The soldiers still decide how they’ll carry it out. Some will follow orders exactly, some will improvise, some might misunderstand completely. And if the general tries to micromanage every little detail? He’ll slow them down and make them less effective.

That’s exactly what President Dwight D. Eisenhower meant when he said, "Plans are useless, but planning is essential." It’s not about locking into a rigid plan—it’s about the process of planning, the clarity of intent, the trust you build with your team. That’s what actually makes success possible. Not the illusion of control.

And look, this isn’t just a battlefield problem. Parents deal with it every single day. You tell your kid to clean their room. They nod. Later, you check, and somehow, the mess is worse than before. Were they being defiant? Not necessarily. They just had a very different idea of what "clean" actually meant.

And here’s the uncomfortable truth: People will always interpret things their own way, no matter how clear you think you are. Control, at least in the way we like to imagine it, is mostly an illusion.

Alright, let’s do a little exercise. Think of someone in your life who’s a total control freak. Maybe a micromanaging boss. A parent who nitpicks everything. A friend who refuses to delegate because "no one does it as well as they do." Got someone in mind? Easy, right? We all know that person.

Now—flip it around. What about you? Are you sure you're not doing the exact same thing? See, that’s where it gets tricky. We all think we’re in control. But are we really? Or are we just gripping the wheel while outside forces actually dictate the ride?

That’s why I always say, thinking outside the box is easy. You know why? Because it means looking at others. That’s outside the box. But real change? That comes when you examine the box you are sitting in. From the outside in. And let’s be honest—that’s the part most people really struggle with.

And hey, sometimes, trying to control everything actually makes things worse. You ever noticed that? The harder you try to hold on, the more things spiral out of control?

There’s this old joke about a guy trying to swat a fly in his kitchen. He swings wildly, knocks over a glass of water, startles the cat, jumps onto the table—bam—spills coffee all over his laptop. The fly? Still happily buzzing around.

That’s control. The more you force it, the more chaos you create.

And this happens in business all the time. I once worked on a project where everything was going smoothly—until we hit the implementation phase. Suddenly, the head of implementation started doing these weird workarounds. I asked him, "Uh… what are you doing?" And he said, "Oh, well, we have a policy that doesn’t allow us to implement it the way we planned."

Turns out, years ago, the company had hired a big consulting firm to increase efficiency. They put all these strict policies in place, meant to make things better. But in reality? Those policies were now completely outdated, and instead of improving efficiency, they were actually slowing everything down. But no one dared change them—because they had been expensive and "official."

So instead of fixing the policy, they just did complicated workarounds. More time, more effort, more frustration. All in the name of "control."

So the real question is: if micromanagement and forcing things don’t actually work, how do we really gain control?

Three things.

First, recognize what you can and can’t control. And here’s the tough truth—what you can control? It’s just you. Your actions. Your thinking. Your emotions. That’s it. You cannot control other people. You can influence them, sure, but you can’t force them.

Second, trust more, dictate less. The more you micromanage, the less control you actually have. People engage more when they feel ownership. Instead of barking orders, set a vision and let people figure out how to get there. That’s what the military calls "mission command." Tell them why you need to take the hill, not how to take it.

And finally, examine your patterns. Are you really in control? Or are you just reacting to fear, frustration, and stress? Self-awareness is everything. But it’s also really hard. It means stepping outside of yourself and questioning your own instincts.

This is what the best leaders—whether generals, CEOs, or parents—understand. They don’t demand blind obedience. They inspire autonomy within clear boundaries.

So here’s the paradox: The less you micromanage, the more control you actually have. Because real control? It’s not about gripping the wheel tighter. It’s about shaping the environment so the right actions happen naturally.

And that, my friends, is the real secret to leadership, parenting, and even self-improvement.
