Welcome to Iconoclast Insights – where we demolish comfortable lies and build uncomfortable truths. I'm your host, and today we're going to talk about something that will make half of you defensive and the other half uncomfortable. Which is Good because that means we're getting somewhere.

## Opening

I was listening to Nickelback's "After the Rain" recently – stay with me here – and one line hit me like a sledgehammer: the idea that everyone talks about patience, but nobody wants to wait. And that's exactly the disease eating away at an entire generation's potential.

We live in the age of the hack. Life hacks, productivity hacks, career hacks. Everyone's looking for the shortcut, the secret door, the express elevator to success. But here's what nobody's telling you: the elevator is taking you to the wrong floor.

## The Elevator Conversation

Let me tell you about a conversation that changed how I see this whole generation. I was talking to a young woman – bright, ambitious, the kind of person who should be leading tomorrow. I asked her how she planned to reach her goals, how she envisioned climbing her mountain.

Her answer was surprisingly simple: "I'll just take the elevator."

Not metaphorically. She literally believed that if there was a faster way up, why wouldn't you take it? And in that moment, I realized we're dealing with a fundamental misunderstanding about what success actually is.

The elevator gets you to the top floor. But it doesn't make you someone who belongs there.

## The Fundamental Problem

Here's the uncomfortable truth: when you take the elevator, you arrive at a destination built by someone else, for someone else's purposes. You're not creating your own summit – you're renting space on theirs.

And what happens when you get there? You find yourself surrounded by everyone else who took the same elevator. What distinguishes you from them? Your LinkedIn profile? Your Instagram aesthetic? Your ability to speak in buzzwords?

Nothing substantial. Nothing that matters. Nothing that lasts.

The elevator creates the illusion of achievement while robbing you of the very thing that makes achievement meaningful: the transformation that happens during the climb.

## Case Study 1: The Copy-Paste Entrepreneur

I know a founder in his early twenties. Smart kid, technically gifted, hungry for success. His business model? Copy a successful American startup and "adapt it for the German market." Sounds strategic, right?

He launched with paid ads, built a funnel, hit some early metrics. For six months, he looked like a genius and I was pretty impressed. Then the algorithm changed. Meta or someone else shifted the rules. His entire business model collapsed overnight.

Why? Because he never learned to build – only to copy. He never developed the deep understanding of value creation that comes from wrestling with real problems. He took the elevator to what looked like success, but he never developed the muscles to stay there when the ground shifted.

He had the appearance of an entrepreneur without the substance of one. And when the market tested him, he failed the test.

## Case Study 2: The Tool Collector

I had the chance to listen into a hiring meeting recently. A young candidate said, with complete confidence, that he wanted to be in leadership within two years. When asked for his reasoning, he answered: "I believe you can get there much faster today if you know the right tools."

Tools. As if leadership were a software download.

This is the elevator mentality in its purest form. The belief that you can acquire the external trappings of success – the frameworks, the methodologies, the productivity apps – and somehow skip the internal development that creates actual leaders.

Leadership isn't a toolkit. It's a way of being that emerges from taking responsibility, making difficult decisions, and learning from failure. You can't hack your way to wisdom. You can't shortcut your way to authority.

## Case Study 3: The Certificate Collector

And then there are the coaches. The ones who collect certifications like merit badges. NLP Practitioner. Resilience Trainer. Human Design Expert. Executive Coach. The list goes on and on.

But when you talk to them, you hear scripts, not insights. You hear methodology, not wisdom. They've learned to speak the language of transformation without ever undergoing it themselves.

They've taken the elevator to the floor labeled "expert," but they've never done the work that creates expertise. They're tourists in the land of mastery, and their clients can feel it -- though, often too late.

## The Generational Divide

Now, the older generation – the 50-plus crowd – they understand this intuitively. They've lived through enough economic cycles, enough industry changes, enough personal setbacks to know that sustainable success isn't built on shortcuts.

They're not necessarily smarter. They're just less naive about the nature of achievement. They've seen what happens when the elevator breaks down, when the quick fix fails, when the hack stops working.

But here's what's interesting: in today's job market, the young and dynamic often get hired over the experienced and substantial. Why is that? Because businesses themselves have become addicted to the elevator mentality. They want rapid growth, instant results, viral success.

But sustainable businesses, like sustainable careers, are built by people who understand that depth beats speed, that consistency beats brilliance, that character beats charisma.

## Let’s take a look at The Viral Trap

We're living through the age of viral success. The TikTok star who becomes a millionaire overnight. The startup that goes from zero to unicorn in eighteen months. The coach who builds a seven-figure business in a year.

These stories aren't lies – they're just incomplete. They show you the elevator ride, not what happens when you get off.

Most viral success is built on trends, not fundamentals. It's optimized for attention, not sustainability. It's designed to work in today's algorithm, not tomorrow's reality.

The harsh truth is that viral success often makes you more fragile, not stronger. You become dependent on external validation, on platform changes, on the whims of an algorithm you don't control.

But Real success – the kind that lasts, the kind that fulfills, the kind that creates actual value – is built slowly, deliberately, and often invisibly.

## The Path Creates the Person

Here's what the elevator riders don't understand: the path creates the person. The obstacles you overcome, the skills you develop, the character you build – these things only happen during the climb.

When you take the elevator, you arrive at the destination, but you're still the same person who stepped in at the bottom. You have the same limitations, the same blind spots, the same capacity for judgment.

But when you climb, you become someone different. Someone who can read the terrain, who knows how to navigate uncertainty, who has the strength to handle what comes next.

The path doesn't just take you to the summit – it makes you someone who deserves to be there.

## After the Rain

This brings me back to that Nickelback song. The central message isn't about patience as a virtue – it's about patience as a necessity. The line about blue skies coming after the rain isn't feel-good philosophy. It's a fundamental truth about how growth actually works.

You can't skip the storm and expect to appreciate the sunshine. You can't avoid the struggle and expect to develop strength. You can't take the elevator and expect to understand the view.

The rain isn't the obstacle to your success – it's the condition for it. The struggle isn't keeping you from your destination – it's creating the person who can handle being there.

## The Real Cost of Shortcuts

Let me be clear about what shortcuts actually cost you. They don't just slow you down – they make you irrelevant.

When you take the elevator, you become interchangeable with everyone else who took the same elevator. You have no unique perspective, no hard-won wisdom, no distinctive capability.

You become a commodity in a world that rewards scarcity. You become predictable in a world that values innovation. You become fragile in a world that demands resilience.

The shortcut doesn't just fail to get you where you want to go – it makes it impossible for you to become who you need to be.

## The Choice

So here's your choice: you can take the elevator, or you can take the path. You can rent someone else's view, or you can earn your own perspective. You can arrive quickly, or you can arrive ready.

The elevator will always be there. It's fast, it's comfortable, it's efficient. But it takes you to someone else's destination, and it leaves you unchanged.

The path is harder. It's slower. It's often lonely. But it takes you to your own summit, and it makes you someone who belongs there.

## Conclusion

The question isn't whether you can take the elevator. The question is whether you want to be the kind of person who needs one.

Real success isn't about reaching the top faster than everyone else. It's about becoming someone who can create value from wherever you are, solve problems that matter, and build something that lasts.

The elevator will get you to the top floor. But only the path will make you someone worth listening to when you get there.

This is Iconoclast Insights. I'm your host, challenging you to take the harder path, because that's where you'll find the person you're meant to become.

Until next time, keep climbing.
