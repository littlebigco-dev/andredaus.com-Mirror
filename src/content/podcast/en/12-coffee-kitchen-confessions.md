Hello and welcome to another episode of Iconoclast Insights.

I am André Daus, and today I want to talk about something that's been bothering me for years because I’ve seen that over and over again – and if you work in any organization that claims to value innovation, this will probably hit close to home.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: In most companies, the best idea doesn't win. The loudest ego does.

And that's not just frustrating – it's devastating for innovation, for people, and ultimately for the bottom line.

## The Coffee Kitchen Phenomenon

Let me paint you a picture that I call the Coffee Kitchen Phenomenon. You're in a meeting. There's a problem to solve, decisions to make. You look around the room and notice something interesting – half the people are barely saying anything. They're nodding, maybe asking a clarifying question, but they're not really contributing.

Then ten minutes later, you're in the coffee kitchen, and suddenly these same quiet people become animated:

"Man, I would have approached that completely differently. The way they're doing it now? It's never going to work."

This happens everywhere. Every single day. And it tells us something crucial: These people have ideas. They have insights. They know what needs to be done.

But they're not sharing it where it matters.

Why?

It's not because they're lazy or don't care. It's because they've learned to make themselves small. They think: "Who am I to have a good idea here?"

That's not a competence problem. That's a culture problem.

## The Real Innovation Killer: Fear of Looking Foolish

When people stay silent in meetings, it's usually not because they have nothing to say. It's because they're afraid – not of the idea itself, but of the reaction to it.

What if my idea isn't good enough?
What if people think I'm trying to show off?
What if I contradict someone who has more influence than me?
What if I'm wrong?

This fear has a name: fear of losing face. And in corporate cultures where mistakes are punished, where criticism gets personal, where hierarchy trumps quality – people shut up.

And silence is the death of real innovation.

But here's what's really insidious about this: We're not just losing ideas. We're training people to stop having them in the first place.

## The Method That Changes Everything

I've found a simple technique that completely transforms these dynamics. It's called:

"Nobody speaks twice until everybody spoke once."

The principle is straightforward: Everyone gets an equal chance to share their thoughts before anyone speaks a second time.

Here's how it works in practice:

First, don't let people just fire off whatever comes to mind immediately. Give everyone two minutes to think. Really think. Let them process the problem, consider different angles.

Then give them another minute to write down their thoughts. One sentence. One concrete idea. Make it specific.

Now go around the room. Everyone reads what they wrote. Importantly: Start with the most junior person in the room. The boss speaks last. If someone has multiple ideas, they have to wait until their next turn.

This changes everything.

Suddenly, people who never speak up are contributing.
Suddenly, you're hearing perspectives that usually get buried.
Suddenly, it's not about who shouts loudest – it's about who thinks best.

## The Ego Problem: When Good Ideas Feel Threatening

But even the best process won't work if there's someone in the room who feels threatened by new ideas.

I've observed this pattern countless times: When a fresh idea emerges, it doesn't get evaluated based on merit. It gets evaluated based on threat level.

Does this idea threaten my influence?
Does it challenge how we've been doing things?
Does it make me look bad for not thinking of it first?
Will I have to give up something I'm proud of?
Will I have to admit my approach wasn't the best?

These are all ego questions. And as long as ego dominates the room, the best ideas will lose to the safest ones.

Here's the brutal reality: Innovation isn't killed by bad ideas. It's killed by good ideas that make insecure people uncomfortable.

## Beyond Process: Creating Psychological Safety

This is why I tell companies: If you want real innovation, stop focusing on innovation workshops and innovation budgets. Start focusing on creating psychological safety.

Innovation isn't about having better brainstorming sessions. It's about creating an environment where people feel safe to be vulnerable, to be wrong, to challenge the status quo without fear of retaliation.

That starts with language. With how leaders respond to ideas – especially ideas that challenge their own thinking. With the willingness to not take yourself too seriously and to accept great ideas even when they don't come from the top.

## The Dogma Trap

And this doesn't just apply in corporate settings. I see this pattern everywhere in society. People make statements like:

"Big tech is evil."
"You can't negotiate with dictators."
"You can't say anything anymore these days."

When I hear these kinds of absolute statements, I don't hear arguments. I hear people who don't want to be bothered with the complexity of actually thinking through difficult problems.

These aren't thoughts – they're dogmas. And dogmas are the enemy of innovation.

Real innovation requires:
- Leaving your comfort zone
- Questioning your worldview
- Engaging with people who make you uncomfortable
- Considering possibilities that threaten your existing beliefs

Because if we keep doing everything the way we've always done it, we'll keep getting the results we've always gotten.

## The German Challenge

Now, I want to address something specific to those of you listening from Germany – though this applies to many cultures that struggle with tall poppy syndrome.

There's an additional cultural layer here that makes innovation even harder: the reflexive suspicion of anyone who stands out or suggests something different.

"Oh, there he goes again with his ideas."
"He thinks he's better than everyone else."

This isn't malicious. It's protective. But it's also innovation poison.

When being different or suggesting change gets you labeled as arrogant or attention-seeking, people learn to keep their best ideas to themselves.

## What Winning Actually Looks Like

So what would it look like if the best ideas actually won?

First, we'd stop confusing innovation with incremental improvement. Most of what companies call innovation is just variation on existing themes. That's fine – small steps matter too. But real innovation requires breakthrough thinking, and breakthrough thinking requires breakthrough courage.

Second, we'd recognize that the person with the best idea might be the most junior person in the room, the quiet one in the corner, or someone from completely outside your industry.

Third, we'd build systems that deliberately counteract ego and hierarchy. Anonymous idea submission. Rotating facilitation. Devil's advocate roles. Anything that separates the quality of the idea from the status of the person sharing it.

## The Innovation Imperative

Here's why this matters more than ever: We're facing challenges that can't be solved with the same thinking that created them. Climate change. Technological disruption. Social inequality. Geopolitical instability.

These problems require fresh thinking. They require the best ideas from everyone, not just the loudest voices or the highest-paid consultants.

But we're systematically suppressing the very diversity of thought we need most.

## Your Action Items

So here's what I want you to do:

If you're a leader: Start paying attention to who's not speaking in your meetings. Create space for those voices. Use the "nobody speaks twice" rule. And when someone challenges your thinking, resist the urge to defend. Ask questions instead.

If you're not in charge: Stop waiting for permission to contribute. Write down your ideas before meetings. Practice articulating them clearly. And when you see someone else struggling to be heard, amplify their voice.

If you're building a culture: Make it safe to be wrong. Celebrate the person who says "I don't know" or "I was wrong" or "That's a better idea than mine."

## The Bottom Line

The best ideas are already in your organization. They're in the minds of people who've learned to stay quiet, who've been trained to make themselves small, who've concluded that their thoughts don't matter.

Your job isn't to generate more ideas. It's to create the conditions where the best ideas can surface, compete on merit, and win.

Because until that happens, you're not innovating. You're just managing egos.
