Welcome to Iconoclast Insights. I'm André Daus, and today we're going to talk about why your organization might not find talent. Spoiler: it's not a labor shortage. It's you.

More specifically, it's your obsession with certificates.

## The Validation Factory

Let me paint you a picture. Right now, someone is clicking through a LinkedIn Learning course with an AI chatbot answering the quiz questions while they watch Netflix. In thirty minutes, they'll have a shiny new certificate to add to their profile. "Certified in Digital Marketing Strategy."

They'll update their LinkedIn. Add it to their resume. Put it in their freelance portfolio. Maybe even add those three magic letters after their name: CDM—Certified Digital Marketer.

They haven't learned anything. They haven't done anything. But they have that certificate. And they'll use it to compete against people who've actually been doing the work for years.

Meanwhile, another person has spent five years actually running successful campaigns, generating millions in revenue, but took two years off to care for a sick parent. Their CV has a gap. Your recruiting department just filtered them out. The freelance marketplace algorithms downranked their profile for "incomplete credentials."

Congratulations. You've just chosen the fraud over the expert. And the fraud knows exactly what they're doing.

This isn't an accident. This is your system working exactly as designed. And it's destroying your ability to compete—whether you're hiring employees or selecting freelancers.

## The Industry Built on Anxiety

We've created an entire industrial complex that exists solely to sell you validation. Not education. Not competence. Validation.

Social Media Influencer Academies. Leadership Certification Programs. Agile Management Coaching Courses. Python Coding Bootcamps. They're all selling the same product: the feeling of progress without the inconvenience of actual achievement.

And here's the brilliant part—for them, not for you—most of these certificates require nothing more than showing up and clicking through. Some let you retake the test until you pass. Others are so trivially easy that an AI can complete them while you sleep.

The assessment is theater. The learning is optional. But the certificate? That's real. That's what goes on the resume. That's what your HR department demands.

You're not filtering for competence. You're filtering for people who are good at collecting meaningless credentials.

## The Recruiter Echo Chamber

Now, I know what some of you are thinking: "But our recruiters know what they're doing. They're professionals."

Let me tell you what I discovered. There's a LinkedIn group for corporate recruiters. I found it because someone posted about it—thought it had substance. So I checked it out. Public posts, so I could read them.

Many discussions were about CVs and certificates. Comment after comment: "Gap-free CV is mandatory." "Proven experience with certificates is non-negotiable." "Without documentation, they have no chance."

And here's the kicker: to join this group, you must be a corporate recruiter. No outside perspectives allowed. No hiring managers who've built successful teams. No entrepreneurs who've found talent in unconventional places. No candidates who could explain what it's like on the other side of your filters.

They've built the perfect echo chamber. And inside it, they're reinforcing each other's biases while simultaneously complaining about talent shortages.

Let that sink in. The same people who can't find good candidates have deliberately isolated themselves from anyone who might tell them why.

## The Competence Paradox

Here's the dirty secret about certificate requirements: they're an admission of incompetence.

When a recruiter demands certificates because they can't evaluate whether someone can do the job, they're confessing they lack the fundamental skill their role requires — namely, recognizing talent.

When a hiring manager needs a third party to validate a candidate's abilities, they're admitting they don't know what good performance looks like in their own organization.

You've outsourced your professional judgment to LinkedIn Learning and Python Academy dot com. 

And you wonder why you keep hiring people who look good on paper but can't solve actual problems.

## The Alphabet Soup Con

Let me tell you about credentials. I've done professional training programs. Each one gave me the "right" to add letters after my name. DTM. CQF. FRM. Pick your acronym.

I could literally add the entire alphabet after my name if I wanted to look impressive.

Here's what those letters don't tell you: whether I can still do the work. I have a Certificate in Quantitative Finance. Haven't worked in that field for a decade. But those three letters still sit there, looking official, implying current competence.

I once had a consultant—young, dynamic, utterly clueless—tell me he could bring a quant into a project to fix a math problem we were discussing. I told him I am a quant. He didn't believe me. Wrong job title, you see. I was a business analyst at the time.

He literally couldn't see the competence sitting in front of him because I wasn't wearing the right label.

That's what your system does. It doesn't just create barriers to entry. It creates barriers to recognition. Your people become so dependent on external validation that they lose the ability to identify expertise when it's right in front of them.

## The Shackleton Standard

In the beginning of the 20th century, Ernest Shackleton needed men for his Antarctic expedition. He didn't write: "Expedition team member needed. Minimum five years' experience. Must have working knowledge of polar conditions. Come work for a fantastic captain with great benefits."

His actual advertisement read: "Men wanted for hazardous journey. Small wages, bitter cold, long months of complete darkness, constant danger, safe return doubtful. Honor and recognition in case of success."

He got exactly the right applicants.

Why? Because he told the truth. He attracted people who wanted exactly what he was offering—hardship, danger, and the chance for something extraordinary.

Your job ads attract people who want generic benefits and comfortable careers. Then you're surprised when they lack initiative and problem-solving ability.

## The Real Solution

So what do you do? How do you break out of this vicious cycle?

Here's the uncomfortable answer: stop trying to fix the system. Instead, exploit it.

While your competitors waste time checking boxes and validating certificates, you have access to an enormous pool of talent they're systematically rejecting. The career-changers. The self-taught experts. The risk-takers. The 50+. The people with gaps in their CVs because they were building businesses, raising families, or simply living non-linear lives.

These experiences often create more capable, resilient employees than those who followed prescribed trajectories.

Be the strategic opposition. Stop asking for irrelevant certificates. Start presenting real problems. Instead of listing required credentials, describe actual challenges.

"Account Executive needed, five years' experience required" becomes "Complex client challenges need creative solutions. Prepare to demonstrate your problem-solving approach."

"Marketing Manager with certifications" becomes "We have a product nobody understands. Show us how you'd change that."

You'll immediately filter for people who think rather than people who collect credentials.

## The Arbitrage Play

This is strategic arbitrage. Your competitors' rigidity becomes your competitive advantage.

Every talented person they reject for lacking certificates is someone you can hire. Every innovative thinker they filter out for an unconventional path is talent flowing directly to you.

The labor shortage isn't real. It's artificial scarcity created by organizations that have forgotten how to recognize competence.

While they complain about not finding good people, you're building teams with the people they're too rigid to see.

And here's the beautiful part: they'll never understand why you're succeeding while they're struggling. They'll probably add more certificate requirements, thinking that will solve the problem. Which just makes your advantage larger.

## The Bottom Line

Let me be direct. Certificates are intellectual costume jewelry—impressive from a distance, worthless up close.

They tell you what someone once attended, not what they can currently do. They measure endurance in clicking through online modules, not capability in solving real problems.

When you require them, you're not protecting your organization from bad hires. You're protecting your recruiters from having to make actual judgments.

And you're losing.

You're losing to competitors who are brave enough to evaluate talent directly. You're losing to startups that can't afford your bureaucratic luxuries and have to recognize competence when they see it. You're losing to every organization that's figured out what you apparently haven't: demonstrated capability beats documented attendance.

Every single time.


## Your Choice

So here's your choice. Keep requiring certificates and gap-free CVs. Keep filtering for people who are good at following prescribed learning paths rather than solving problems. Keep wondering why you can't find good people while talent walks past you wearing the wrong labels.

Or stop playing their game entirely.

The question isn't whether you can afford to ignore certificates in your hiring. The question is whether you can afford to keep using them while someone smarter captures all the talent you're filtering out.

Next time you're hiring, ask yourself one simple question: Am I looking for someone who can do the job, or someone who can document that they've learned about doing the job?

Your answer will determine whether you solve your talent shortage or perpetuate it.


That's today's Iconoclast Insight. Challenge the system, capture the talent, and watch your competitors wonder why they can't find good people while you're building exceptional teams.

Until next time, keep questioning everything—especially what everyone else certifies as truth.
