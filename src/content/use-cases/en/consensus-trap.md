## The Problem

A six-person leadership team at a mid-size B2B software company had noticed something they initially took as a good sign: their strategy meetings ran smoothly. Decisions were reached quickly. There was no conflict.

Six months later, they were reviewing a market entry that had underperformed significantly — a decision that had been reached in a 45-minute meeting with no dissent.

When the CEO reviewed the meeting notes, she noticed something: every concern that had been raised in preparatory emails had been absent from the meeting discussion. The objections existed. They just never made it into the room.

## The Pattern

This is the consensus trap: the social dynamics of a senior leadership team — built over years of working together, shaped by implicit hierarchies and relationship costs — operate as a filter on what gets said.

It doesn't require bad faith. It requires only that the people in the room accurately calculate that surfacing their objection carries a cost (friction, attribution, being seen as resistant) that outweighs the benefit (possibly improving a decision that will probably be made anyway).

When this calculation is made consistently, the leadership team develops a systematic blind spot: they stop hearing the dissent that exists, and they start confusing the absence of voiced objection with the absence of risk.

## The Intervention

Over two 90-minute Clarity Sessions, we worked through the specific decision and the dynamics that had led to it.

The first session mapped the decision: what assumptions had been treated as given, what scenarios hadn't been modelled, and what the best case against the decision would have been.

The second session examined the team dynamic: not what was said, but what the people in the room were likely thinking and not saying, and why.

The output wasn't a reversal of the decision. It was a set of early indicators — specific metrics and milestones — that would flag whether the assumptions underlying the decision were holding. And it was a different kind of meeting: one where the CEO opened by asking for the strongest argument against the plan, before asking anyone to defend it.

## What Changed

The team now runs a structured adversarial review as part of every major strategic decision. One person is assigned to make the best case against the proposed direction before discussion begins. The quality of their decisions hasn't improved because they reach agreement more slowly — it's improved because the agreement they reach reflects actual analysis rather than the accumulated social cost of continued objection.
