## The Investment Looked Sound

A sales director at a €60M industrial components manufacturer watches a vendor demo for an AI-powered customer service and order-management platform. The system answers complex product queries in seconds. It routes orders without human input. The interface is clean. The numbers in the business case — 65% autonomous resolution, payback in 18 months, €190K in annual savings — are specific enough to feel researched.

The proposal goes to the executive team. Nobody pushes back. The vendor has reference customers. The technology is credible. The competitive pressure is real — two regional competitors have announced similar deployments in the past year.

The CEO approves a €380K first-year commitment. Implementation begins.

## What Everyone Else Did

The company brought in a consulting firm to manage the rollout. They ran a vendor selection process that validated the choice already made. They produced an implementation roadmap. They held weekly status meetings and managed stakeholder communication.

Nobody asked the question that mattered: does the scenario in the demo bear any resemblance to this company's actual situation?

The answer was no. But nobody found that out until month eight.

## The Assumptions Nobody Tested

The demo used the vendor's curated showcase data — clean, structured, consistently formatted. The manufacturer's order data lived in a legacy ERP system that had been extended, patched, and modified over fifteen years. Fields were inconsistently populated. Product codes had been deprecated and reused. The AI had no usable foundation.

The vendor's reference customers were in consumer e-commerce. Their "complex inquiries" were questions about delivery dates and return policies. This manufacturer's customers called about custom specifications, tolerances, and substitution options that required a human with product knowledge. The 65% autonomous resolution figure was never going to transfer.

The integration timeline was "six to eight weeks" in the proposal. It took seven months. The ERP integration was classified as "standard" without anyone verifying what standard meant for a system of this age and configuration.

Each of these was a testable assumption. None of them were tested before the contract was signed.

## What Due Diligence Actually Looks Like

The work starts before the contract, not after it.

A five-day assessment would have surfaced all three failure points. Day one: map the assumptions the vendor's business case depends on. Not the headline numbers — the premises underneath them. What does "65% autonomous resolution" require to be true about data quality, query type, and integration depth?

Day two and three: test those premises against the actual situation. Pull a sample of last quarter's customer inquiries. Classify them by type. Compare against the vendor's reference customer profiles. Look at the ERP schema and ask how long a real integration has taken for equivalent systems.

Day four: write the gap analysis. Not a risk register — a verdict. Which assumptions hold, which don't, and what the realistic outcome range looks like if you proceed.

Day five: the recommendation. In this case: don't sign the contract as proposed. Negotiate a paid pilot — ninety days, with the manufacturer's actual data, against a pre-agreed success threshold. If the vendor won't do that, treat the refusal as information.

The manufacturer would have saved €380K. Or they would have signed a contract with actual evidence behind it.

Either outcome is better than what happened.

*The question isn't whether AI can do what the demo showed. It's whether it can do that with your data, your queries, and your systems. That's a different question — and it has a knowable answer before you commit the budget.*
