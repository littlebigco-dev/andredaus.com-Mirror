Confirmation bias is the tendency to search for, interpret, favour, and recall information in a way that confirms or supports one's prior beliefs or values. It is one of the most pervasive cognitive biases in strategic decision-making and one of the most difficult to correct.

## Why It Matters in Strategy

In organizational contexts, confirmation bias operates at multiple levels simultaneously. Individual leaders filter incoming information through existing beliefs. Teams create environments where confirming information is rewarded and disconfirming information is suppressed. Over time, the organization develops a systematic blind spot for evidence that contradicts its strategic assumptions.

The insidious quality of confirmation bias is that it feels like rigour. You're "testing" your hypothesis by looking at evidence. You're "listening to feedback" by attending to customer responses. What's happening is the opposite: you're using the appearance of evidence-gathering to reinforce beliefs you already hold.

## Common Manifestations

**In market analysis:** Selecting data sources, time ranges, or customer segments that support the existing market thesis while de-prioritizing data that complicates it.

**In competitive assessment:** Interpreting competitor moves as confirming your own strategy's validity rather than as signals worth examining on their own terms.

**In team feedback loops:** Cultivating advisors and board members who share your fundamental assumptions, and attributing their agreement to their expertise rather than to the similarity of their priors.

**In post-mortems:** When things go wrong, attributing failure to external factors or execution while leaving the underlying strategic assumptions intact.

## The Correction

The most reliable correction for confirmation bias is structured exposure to disconfirming evidence from someone with no stake in confirming your current view. This is why external challenge — genuine adversarial examination rather than validation — is so valuable at the strategic level.

The goal isn't to find evidence that your strategy is wrong. It's to find the best possible case against it, take that case seriously, and then decide what it means for your thinking.

## Related Concepts

Confirmation bias interacts heavily with groupthink, the planning fallacy, and motivated reasoning. In organizational settings, it is almost always amplified by hierarchy: the higher someone's position, the less likely they are to receive disconfirming information in undistorted form.
