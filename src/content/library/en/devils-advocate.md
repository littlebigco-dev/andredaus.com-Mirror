The Devil's Advocate is a structured facilitation role in which one or more participants are explicitly tasked with arguing against the prevailing position, regardless of their own views. The term originates from the Catholic Church's formal process for evaluating candidates for sainthood, where an advocate was assigned to argue against canonization.

## The Problem It Addresses

In groups where social dynamics suppress dissent, valuable objections go unvoiced. The Devil's Advocate role creates a structural permission for challenge that doesn't require any individual to risk the social cost of genuine opposition.

This is both its primary value and its most significant limitation.

## How It Works

The role is most effective when:

1. **Clearly assigned before the discussion begins** — not invoked retroactively to explain unwelcome criticism
2. **Rotated across team members** — preventing any one person from becoming the perpetual contrarian
3. **Separated from personal attribution** — the role-holder is arguing a position, not expressing a view
4. **Given equal time and reception** — the challenge must be taken seriously, not performed and dismissed

The outputs of Devil's Advocate facilitation are most useful not as objections to be refuted but as stress-tests: *if this argument were partially correct, what would that mean for our plan?*

## Limitations

The Devil's Advocate is a simulation of opposition, not opposition itself. Its most important limitation is that it is performative: a skilled Devil's Advocate argues well for positions they don't hold, but the quality of challenge depends on the role-holder's creativity and willingness to engage seriously with unfamiliar positions.

The more fundamental issue is that genuine strategic risk often doesn't come from arguments that team members could construct if asked — it comes from perspectives, information, and frameworks that are not represented in the room at all. The Devil's Advocate addresses vocal suppression; it doesn't address epistemic blind spots.

## When to Use It

Devil's Advocate facilitation is most useful in groups with established social norms that suppress overt disagreement — teams with strong hierarchical dynamics, long-tenured leadership, or cultures that conflate cohesion with correctness.

It is less useful in groups that already have high tolerance for dissent, and actively counterproductive if the role is deployed defensively — as a way of appearing to have challenged a decision that was effectively predetermined.
