The Five Whys is a root cause analysis technique developed within the Toyota Production System. The method involves iterating through causal chains by asking "why" five times in succession — each answer forming the basis of the next question — until a root cause is identified.

## The Core Mechanic

The power of the method lies in its resistance to symptom-level thinking. Most problem-solving stops at the first or second causal layer — the level that's visible, attributable, and actionable in the near term. The Five Whys forces continued interrogation until a structural or systemic cause emerges.

**Example chain:**
1. *The customer is churning.* Why?
2. *The product isn't meeting their expectation.* Why?
3. *We promised a capability we haven't fully built.* Why?
4. *Sales made commitments before product validated feasibility.* Why?
5. *Sales and product have no shared process for evaluating scope before commitment.*

The root cause is a structural misalignment between sales and product. The symptom was churn. Treating the symptom — improving onboarding, adding features — would not address the root.

## Limitations and Extensions

The Five Whys is a heuristic, not an algorithm. Several important limitations:

**Linear causality:** The method assumes a single causal chain. Real organizational problems often have multiple interacting causes. When this is the case, the Five Whys should be run on multiple parallel chains rather than a single thread.

**Stopping too early:** The number five is approximate. Some problems require more iterations; others fewer. The relevant signal is whether you've arrived at a cause that is structural or systemic — something you can change that would prevent the problem at the root rather than at the surface.

**Retrospective bias:** The method works best when applied carefully with multiple perspectives. Without this, the causal chain reflects the beliefs of whoever is asking the questions rather than the actual structure of the problem.

## Application in Strategic Contexts

In strategic decision-making, the Five Whys is most useful for interrogating persistent problems — situations that have been addressed but keep returning. If a strategic challenge keeps reappearing despite interventions, the interventions are likely aimed at the wrong causal level.

The discipline the method instils — *not accepting the first plausible explanation* — is more broadly applicable than the specific technique. It's a corrective for the common tendency to act on the first interpretation of a problem that feels coherent.
