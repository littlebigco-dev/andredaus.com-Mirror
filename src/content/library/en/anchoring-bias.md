The first number in the room wins. Not because it's right. Because it's first.

Anchoring bias is the cognitive tendency to fix on an initial piece of information — a price, a projection, a sentence length — and adjust from it rather than from the evidence. Every subsequent judgment gravitates toward that starting point, regardless of whether it belongs there. The adjustment almost always falls short. We land closer to the anchor than the facts warrant.

## How It Works

Tversky and Kahneman demonstrated the mechanism in 1974. Participants asked to estimate what percentage of African nations were in the UN gave consistently higher answers when a wheel had been spun to land on 65 than when it landed on 10 — even though the wheel was obviously random and obviously irrelevant. The anchor didn't need to be credible. It just needed to arrive first.

The brain processes the anchor through two pathways. The first is selective accessibility: the anchor primes related information into working memory, so anchor-consistent evidence becomes easier to retrieve than anchor-inconsistent evidence. The second is insufficient adjustment: rather than building an estimate independently, we start at the anchor and move. But the movement stops too early. We land in the vicinity of where we started.

This isn't a failure of intelligence. It happens to experienced professionals. It happens to people who have been explicitly warned about it. Kahneman's research consistently showed that knowing about anchoring provides only marginal protection against it.

## Where It Shows Up in Strategy

In boardrooms, the first figure in a financial model becomes the implicit benchmark. Subsequent revisions are argued as deviations from it rather than independent assessments. A budget presented at £4 million anchors the conversation there — even if the honest independent answer would have been £2.5 million or £6 million.

In acquisition negotiations, the opening bid sets the range. Professional deal-makers understand this. The side that names the first number chooses the gravitational centre of the negotiation. A seller who anchors high forces the buyer to argue downward from an inflated baseline. A buyer who anchors low reframes what a reasonable price looks like.

In performance reviews, last quarter's results anchor expectations for next quarter. In hiring, the salary a candidate last held anchors what feels appropriate to offer next. In legal settings, Guthrie, Rachlinski, and Wistrich found that judges — experienced, trained, supposedly insulated from such effects — were influenced by prosecutors' sentencing recommendations in ways that tracked the anchor rather than the merits.

Strategic planning is particularly vulnerable. When a leadership team opens a planning cycle by reviewing the prior year's strategy, that strategy becomes the anchor. The question shifts from 'what should we do?' to 'how much should we change what we're already doing?' Those are different questions, and they produce different answers.

## The Structural Problem

Anchoring isn't a problem that individuals can think their way out of. The research is clear on this. Awareness raises the ceiling slightly. It doesn't remove the floor.

Organisations compound the effect by structuring their decision processes in ways that guarantee early exposure to a reference point. A board pack that opens with management's recommended option anchors directors before they've assessed the alternatives. A market entry discussion that begins with 'we were thinking around a 15% market share target' has already decided more than it realises.

The deeper issue is that anchoring hides inside what looks like consensus. The room agrees on a number. Nobody notices that the agreement was shaped entirely by whoever spoke first. That isn't consensus. That's arithmetic around an anchor.

## How Strategic Opposition Counteracts It

The structural fix is adversarial process, not awareness training. An external challenger who enters a decision without prior exposure to the anchor — and whose explicit role is to question rather than build on what's already been framed — disrupts the gravitational pull before it sets.

Practically, this means generating independent estimates before any reference point is introduced. If the team produces its own valuation before the seller names a price, the negotiation has two anchors, not one. The same principle applies to strategy: independent challenge that begins from first principles, not from the existing plan, produces genuinely different conclusions.

Pre-mortems, red teams, and structured devil's advocacy all work by the same mechanism — they force reasoning that starts somewhere other than where the anchor was planted. The adversarial position doesn't just identify flaws. It resets the reference point.

## The Diagnostic Question

In the last significant decision your organisation made, who introduced the first number — and did anyone in the room build their position from independent analysis, or from adjusting away from it?
