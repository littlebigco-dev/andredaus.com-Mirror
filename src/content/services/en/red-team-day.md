## The problem it solves

Most strategy work produces alignment. Teams come in with different positions and leave agreeing on a direction. That feels like progress.

It often isn't.

What looks like alignment is frequently the outcome of a room that has stopped being honest with itself — where challenge feels disloyal, dissent gets managed rather than heard, and the strongest objections live in the conversations that happen after the meeting, not in it.

The Red Team Day is not designed to produce alignment. It's designed to produce clarity — about what your strategy actually depends on, and whether those dependencies can hold.

### What actually happens

Your leadership team arrives with a real strategic situation: a direction you're moving in, a decision you're navigating, a plan that's already in motion but hasn't been pressure-tested.

I run structured adversarial facilitation. Not debate. Not brainstorming. A disciplined process of surfacing the load-bearing assumptions in your current thinking, then stress-testing each one against the scenarios where it breaks.

By the end, your team has a documented assumption map — a complete inventory of what your strategy depends on being true, ranked by the damage if it isn't.

### Half-day vs full-day

The half-day format works for a single focused question or initiative. The full-day format covers a broader strategic direction and includes working sessions to test high-priority assumptions, not just name them.

Both formats are available remotely or in-person. In-person is strongly preferred for the full-day format.
