## The noise isn't the problem. The question is.

Something happened in the last four months that is worth paying attention to — not because of what it reveals about AI, but because of what it reveals about how people think under pressure.

The feeds filled up. Every newsletter, every conference keynote, every leadership offsite agenda. The tone shifted from cautious curiosity to something closer to existential alarm. Entire industries declared cooked. Business models declared obsolete. The humans had a good run.

And then the temperature checks started. Industry figures, analysts, thought leaders — people with genuine knowledge and genuine audiences — began polling their communities and their boards. *Has demand changed? Is our positioning still relevant? Are we cooked?*

## Reasonable questions, asked in good faith. But still the wrong questions.

"Are we cooked?" is a fear-shaped question. It presupposes that your organisation's future is something that happens *to* you — decided somewhere upstream by forces you cannot influence. It frames the situation as a verdict rather than a variable. And it directs attention toward the noise instead of toward the only thing that actually matters: what problem you are solving, for whom, and why that matters to them.

This is not a minor framing issue. The question you ask determines the answer you find. Ask "are we cooked?" and you will spend your energy scanning for evidence of either doom or survival — and you will find plenty of both, because the noise is loud enough to support any conclusion you are already leaning toward. Ask "what problem am I actually solving?" and you start doing work that compounds.

> The problem, as it almost always turns out, is rarely the problem you think it is.

Most of the uncertainty I observe in organisations right now is not really about AI. AI is the current surface. Underneath it is something older: a significant number of businesses — and the leaders running them — that never had a precise answer to the foundational questions. What do we actually do? What does it produce for the people who pay for it? What would be lost if we stopped?

When you have clear answers to those questions, AI is a capability shift. It changes the speed of analysis, the reach of your reasoning, the cost of certain kinds of execution. That is worth understanding and integrating deliberately.

When you do not have clear answers to those questions, AI is a threat. Not because it will replace your organisation specifically, but because it will replace the blur. The vague middle ground of "we provide solutions" or "we support our clients' growth" or "we handle the complexity so they don't have to" — that territory shrinks fast when AI commoditises the execution layer. If what you sell is primarily execution dressed up as strategy, that is a real problem. But it is not an AI problem. It was always going to become a problem. AI is simply accelerating the timeline.

> This is not comfortable to say. But comfortable is not what this situation calls for.

The "everything is dead" genre of content has been with us for as long as there has been content. E-commerce was going to kill retail. Software was going to kill professional services. Outsourcing was going to eliminate middle management. Automation was going to end blue-collar work. Each wave produced the same pattern: loud declarations, significant panic, a period of genuine disruption, and then a new equilibrium that looked different from what came before — but still required skilled people with clear thinking and a precise understanding of the problem they were solving.

The equilibrium is not guaranteed to include you. That is the honest part. But whether it does depends far more on the clarity of your positioning and the depth of your thinking than on the sophistication of any tool — including the ones currently making the most noise.

What AI has done — and this is worth sitting with — is raise the cost of strategic vagueness. When execution was slow and expensive, there was room to be imprecise about what you were actually doing and why. The friction bought you time. Clients and customers often could not easily compare alternatives, could not quickly test assumptions, could not see through the complexity you were being paid to manage.

That friction is going away. Not all at once, not in every sector simultaneously, but directionally and irreversibly. Which means the organisations that will navigate this well are not the ones with the best AI tools. They are the ones who can answer, with precision and without hesitation, what they do, who they do it for, and why that is genuinely hard to replace.

If you can answer that, the conversation about AI becomes straightforward: which capabilities does it expand, and how do you integrate them without losing what makes your work valuable?

If you cannot answer that, the tools are a distraction from the actual work that needs doing.

## The question worth asking is not whether you are cooked.

It is whether you ever had a clear enough answer to what you were cooking in the first place.

If you did — if you can articulate the problem you solve, the person who has it, and what changes for them when you solve it — then what you have in front of you is not a threat. It is an expansion of your capacity to do that work better and further.

If you did not, then now is the right time to find out. Not because AI forced the question. Because the question was always there, and the noise finally got loud enough that ignoring it costs more than answering it.

> *The problem is rarely the problem.*  
> *The question, though — the question matters enormously.*
