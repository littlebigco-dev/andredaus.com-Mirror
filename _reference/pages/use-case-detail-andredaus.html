Problem The situation as most organisations describe it

The Old Perimeter Is Dead. Most Organisations Haven't Noticed.

For decades, the cybersecurity playbook was straightforward: build a wall. Install antivirus, configure a firewall, brief the IT team. If the wall held, you were secure. If it didn't, you called someone who understood firewalls better than you did.

The assumption this case challenges

Cybersecurity is a technical problem that requires a technical solution.

This assumption was true when your company's data lived in one place, on one network, accessed by people in one building. That world no longer exists — and the assumption calcified into orthodoxy before anyone noticed the world had changed.

The attack surface expanded quietly and completely. Company data now lives across dozens of cloud services. Employees work from home, from airports, from coffee shops, on personal devices running personal software. The perimeter that security teams were hired to defend dissolved over a decade of digital transformation — and most security strategies didn't dissolve with it.

"Today's attacks don't target computers. They target people. A sophisticated campaign might start with researching an employee on LinkedIn and end with access to your entire network."

This is why the framing "cybersecurity is an IT problem" is not just wrong — it's actively dangerous. It delegates responsibility for a company-wide vulnerability to a team that controls, at most, a fraction of the actual risk surface. And it lets everyone else opt out.

What a modern attack actually looks like

It doesn't start with a piece of code. It starts with research. An attacker reads your company's LinkedIn page, identifies a new employee in finance, crafts a convincing email from what looks like your CFO's address, and waits. The employee clicks. The attacker is in. Nothing in your firewall configuration stops this. Because the vulnerability isn't technical — it's human.

The companies that survive the current threat landscape aren't necessarily the ones with the biggest security budgets. They're the ones that treat security as a business risk, managed like any other, embedded at every level. Not something the "tech people" handle. Something everyone handles.

A
Agitate What it actually costs — and why conventional responses compound it

When Giants Fall: Real Failures, Real Costs

In 2017, Maersk's IT systems went down. Not crashed — went down completely. The NotPetya attack forced the world's largest shipping company to rebuild 4,000 servers and 45,000 PCs from scratch. Operations that normally moved millions of tonnes of cargo a day ran on pen and paper. Estimated cost: $300 million. For a single incident.

$300M

Maersk's estimated cost from the NotPetya attack — a breach that started not with sophisticated technical exploitation, but with a compromised software update from a third-party supplier. Human decisions in a supply chain created the vulnerability.

Source: Wired, 2018

The European Medicines Agency had vaccine data not just stolen but manipulated before being leaked. Spotify — a technology company — couldn't prevent breaches of user data. These aren't organisations that neglected security. They invested in it. They had security teams, security budgets, security frameworks. And they still failed.

The scale of what's coming

By 2025, there will be approximately 30.9 billion IoT devices globally. Each one is a potential entry point — a door that needs to be locked. Meanwhile, Europe alone is short 200,000 cybersecurity professionals. The defenders are outnumbered before the game starts.

+150%

Ransomware attacks increased year-on-year — and European organisations now pay €1.5 million on average per incident. By 2025, cybercrime is projected to cost €10.5 trillion annually. More than the GDP of every country except the US and China.

Multiple sources: Verizon DBIR, Sophos, Eurostat

The Security Theater problem

Most organisations respond to this landscape the same way. They buy more tools. The average enterprise now runs 60–80 security products — most of which don't integrate properly, generating thousands of alerts that security teams couldn't process if they worked 24 hours a day. Meanwhile, compliance frameworks get completed: policies drafted, checklists ticked, audit reports filed. And actual security barely improves.

"It's like installing an expensive alarm system and leaving the key under the doormat. The technical solution creates an illusion of security while the actual vulnerability remains unaddressed."

This is Security Theater. It looks like security. It generates impressive documentation. Consulting firms love it because it generates impressive projects. But it doesn't address the thing that Verizon's data has been saying clearly for years: 85% of breaches involve a human component. The tools are solving a fraction of the problem while the majority of the risk goes unaddressed.

The second assumption killing your security posture

More tools equals more security.

At some point — and most organisations crossed it years ago — additional security tooling starts actively degrading security. Alert fatigue means genuine threats get missed. Integration failures create gaps. Complexity creates surface area. The belief that security is proportional to security spend is the assumption that the entire industry profits from you holding.

Solution The Strategic Opposition reframe

Security Through Organisational Cohesion: The Counterintuitive Shield

The most effective cybersecurity strategy isn't more technology or stricter policies. It's a different mental model for what security is.

Security isn't a feature you install. It's a behaviour pattern that either exists in an organisation's culture or it doesn't. No tool enforces that. No compliance framework creates it. It gets built — deliberately, through how organisations communicate, how they respond to mistakes, and how they distribute responsibility for risk.

The Strategic Opposition reframe

Stop treating your people as the weakest link. They are your most adaptable security system.

A technical control responds to known threats the way it was programmed to respond. A person, properly empowered and properly supported, can identify an attack that no tool has ever seen before. The question isn't how to protect your organisation from its people — it's how to turn its people into active defenders.

What this looks like in practice

Forward-thinking organisations are embedding security consciousness throughout their culture rather than siloing it in a specialised function. This means mapping existing informal networks — identifying who people actually go to for help, regardless of the org chart — and making those natural influencers security champions. Not by giving them compliance checklists, but by equipping them to communicate clearly about risk in the language of their specific teams.

It means integrating security into existing meeting rhythms rather than scheduling parallel security trainings that people find reasons to avoid. It means cross-functional teams running simplified threat-modelling exercises focused on their actual business processes — not abstract technical vulnerability taxonomies. It means shifting security metrics from "vulnerabilities patched" to "time to detect and respond to a simulated attack."

"The security team stops being the department that says no. They become the function that helps every other department achieve its objectives without creating catastrophic risk."

Critically: this approach doesn't require massive new investment. It requires reconfiguring the resources and relationships you already have — which is what makes it both counterintuitive and effective. The organisations spending the most on security are often not the most secure ones. The organisations where security is genuinely everyone's responsibility usually are.

The Strategic Opposition engagement

An Assumption Hunting engagement applied to this problem starts by surfacing what your organisation currently believes about its security posture — not what's in the CISO's presentation to the board, but what people actually believe at the operational level. Those beliefs are the load-bearing assumptions your current security strategy depends on. Most of them have never been stress-tested.

From there, the engagement maps the gap between where your actual risk lives (overwhelmingly: in human behaviour, in communication patterns, in informal processes) and where your current security investment is concentrated (overwhelmingly: in tools and compliance). That gap is where the work is.

The deliverable isn't a new security policy. It's a shift in how the organisation thinks about security — which is the only change that actually lasts.
